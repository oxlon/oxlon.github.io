Reading the April FOMC: what the press conference said that the statement did not.

A word-level diff between the prepared remarks and the answered Q&A. The gap is where the policy signal actually lives — and in April, the gap is wider than in any meeting since late 2023.

FOMC meetings produce two artefacts: the statement, which is a negotiated document the committee agrees on, and the press conference, where the Chair speaks extemporaneously within constraints the committee cannot fully police. When the two agree, the signal is settled. When they disagree, the signal is being written in real time — and the press conference is usually the version that matters.

Our text-as-data pipeline has tracked this gap for every FOMC meeting since 2015. On most days it is small, a few turns of phrase. On 16 April 2026, it was substantial. This is a short note on what the difference says.

What the statement said

The prepared statement made three minor edits relative to March. The language on the balance of risks was nudged from "elevated" to "elevated but more balanced". The reference to "labour-market imbalances" was dropped entirely. The forward-guidance paragraph was left unchanged — which, by itself, would have been the story on any other Wednesday.

Statement · paragraph 3 · diff vs March The risks to the outlook remain elevatedelevated but more balanced. Labour-market imbalances persist and Inflation remains above the Committee's longer-run objective…

If a market participant read only the statement, the reasonable inference would be a small, managed softening. The policy path implied was roughly unchanged. A rate cut remained priced for the autumn.

What the Chair also said

The press conference, which ran 48 minutes, told a different story. Not in the prepared remarks — those tracked the statement — but in the Q&A, where four specific exchanges revised the direction of travel.

In response to a question on the pace of services disinflation, the Chair answered in a way that, on our reading, was materially more dovish than the statement allowed for:

The disinflationary trajectory in core services has, frankly, exceeded what the committee expected when it last met. Several of us have begun to re-examine the timing of further accommodation on the basis of that data.

The construction is unusual in two respects. Several of us is a formulation the Chair uses only when signalling internal movement that the committee has not yet formalised. And the verb re-examine the timing, rather than the boilerplate calibrate the stance, is closer in register to pre-cut language in the 2019 and 2024 cycles than to the cautious reading that had dominated March.

Three other exchanges amplified the signal:

  • Asked about labour-market slack, the Chair described the market as having “returned to roughly neutral” — a characterisation not present in the statement, which dropped the slack language entirely rather than redefining it.
  • On the Committee’s reaction function, the Chair specifically declined to reaffirm the phrase meaningfully above target that has anchored policy language for the past four meetings. Omission, in that specific place, reads as repositioning.
  • In the final answer of the session, on the balance-sheet runoff, the Chair said “the end of reductions is closer than it is further” — again, not in the statement, and a concrete step beyond March’s reference to the runoff as “well-advanced”.

Why this matters for the path

Taken individually, each of these exchanges is the kind of imprecision that any extended press conference produces. Taken together, they form a pattern: the Chair reaching for language that the committee would not, at this meeting, put into the statement. That is characteristic of a committee in the last meeting before a decision is effectively made — not the last meeting before a decision is announced, which is different and later.

Markets caught this, though partially. Front-end rates fell 6 basis points in the hour after the press conference opened, then gave back about half of that as the statement was re-read. The delta between what a statement-only reader would conclude and what a press-conference-attentive reader would conclude is, for this meeting, around 12–14 basis points in the implied June rate path. That is our estimate of the value of reading beyond the statement for this particular Wednesday.

Takeaway

When the statement holds steady and the press conference doesn’t, the press conference is the policy. On April’s read, the committee is closer to its first cut than March’s statement, taken alone, would suggest.

A note on method

The diff shown above is not a manual transcript — it is the output of a pipeline that aligns prepared remarks with Q&A answers, tokens-level, across every FOMC meeting in our corpus (106 meetings, 2007–present). The specific metric we use to score a “gap-widening” meeting combines four components: paragraph-level semantic divergence, committee-pronoun frequency, hedging-adverb density, and the presence of calibration verbs that are outside the rolling vocabulary of the statement. April’s meeting scored in the 88th percentile of the sample.

The methodology is described in full in our working paper on FOMC transcript-statement gaps, which also lays out the limits of the approach and the cases (and there are several) where the method misreads a quiet meeting as a loud one. We publish the score after every meeting, along with a one-paragraph reading.

Subscribers to The Oxlon Memo receive the score within 36 hours of each meeting, together with the four or five exchanges that moved the number. It is the shortest route we know of to a reading that treats the press conference as the primary document — which, most of the time, it is.
