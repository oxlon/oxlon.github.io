Thesis · why we exist
i.

Most AI firms know machine learning. Most economics firms know economics. The overlap — where rigorous method and production engineering live under one roof — is where the work that matters sits.

Oxlon was founded to be a firm that sits in the overlap: small, senior, and accountable for the number.

Every engagement is led by one of us. Every model we ship is one we would defend in front of a monetary policy committee — because we have been in that room.

Commitments

Three lines
we won’t cross.

These are not values in a handbook. They are the operational rules we hold ourselves to before taking on any engagement — and the ones we decline work against.

i.

Institutional discipline.

Our engineering meets the documentation, governance and reproducibility bar that central banks apply internally — not as an aspiration, but as the floor. Every number we produce can be traced to its data vintage, its model version, and the economist who signed it. If we cannot defend it on paper, we do not ship it.

ii.

Economics, not novelty.

We combine modern machine learning with classical macroeconomic method. New techniques are used where they earn their place — never to replace a clearer story with a fancier one. A local projection that answers the question beats a neural network that does not. We tell you when the simpler method is the right method.

iii.

A bench that has shipped.

The senior name on the proposal writes the code. Our founders have built production AI systems and published peer-reviewed macroeconomic research. Work is not delegated to juniors with a partner watching from a distance. You speak to the economist doing the modelling.

How we got here

Oxford and London.
Then Baku.
Then, gradually, further.

Oxlon began in late 2024 as a working partnership between three former academics who had each, separately, reached the same conclusion — that the gap between what AI can now do, and what institutions know how to use, has become the binding constraint on serious economic work.

The firm was set up in Oxford, where Fatih had been teaching, with operations in London and an early engagement in Baku — where we now deploy EconAI inside the Ministry of Economy of the Republic of Azerbaijan. That project was the first test of the full thesis: that on-premise AI, built around a classical econometric core and reviewed by a real research team, is a materially better surface for policy work than anything currently on the market.

We grew deliberately from there. A second research track on the Economics of AI opened in 2025, and the Atlas learning programme ran its first executive cohort at Brasenose in the autumn. New engagements are taken selectively, on the basis of fit rather than fee.

We are not a fast-scaling firm. The work we do is the work we can defend — and defensible work, at our bar, takes the time it takes.
