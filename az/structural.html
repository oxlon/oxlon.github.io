Structural Models

The econometric models describe the data; the structural models explain it. A micro-founded New-Keynesian DSGE and an input-output block provide theory-consistent cross-checks and support the policy counterfactuals that reduced-form models cannot address.

Why a structural model

Reduced-form regressions and vector autoregressions summarise historical correlations, but they are silent on the effect of a policy that has no historical precedent, and they are vulnerable to the Lucas critique. A dynamic stochastic general equilibrium (DSGE) model is built from the optimising behaviour of households and firms, so its parameters are structural and its responses can be interpreted as causal under the stated assumptions. The canonical three-equation New-Keynesian model (Galí, 2008) is the standard such framework and the natural complement to the empirical work here.

A three-equation New-Keynesian model

The model comprises a forward-looking investment-saving relation (from the household's consumption-saving choice), a New-Keynesian Phillips curve (from staggered price-setting), and a Taylor rule for monetary policy. It is solved under rational expectations by the method of undetermined coefficients.

$$ \tilde y_t = \mathbb{E}_t \tilde y_{t+1} - \tfrac{1}{\sigma}\big(i_t-\mathbb{E}_t\pi_{t+1}-r^n_t\big)+\varepsilon^d_t $$(D1)
$$ \pi_t=\beta\,\mathbb{E}_t\pi_{t+1}+\kappa\,\tilde y_t+\varepsilon^s_t $$(D2)
$$ i_t=\phi_\pi\,\pi_t+\phi_y\,\tilde y_t+\varepsilon^m_t $$(D3)

where ỹ is the output gap, π inflation, i the nominal policy rate and rn the natural real rate; 𝔼t denotes the rational expectation and εd, εs, εm are the demand, cost-push and monetary shocks. The parameters are σ (the intertemporal elasticity of substitution), β (the discount factor), κ (the slope of the Phillips curve) and φπ, φy (the policy responses to inflation and to the output gap).

Calibrated at conventional values for a small economy: sigma = 1.0, beta = 0.99, kappa = 0.13, phi_pi = 1.5, phi_y = 0.125, rho = 0.5. The discount factor and the inflation-response coefficient follow the standard literature; the slope of the Phillips curve and the interest-elasticity are set to the mid-range of published estimates.

The responses are correctly signed and economically interpretable. A monetary tightening lowers output and inflation while raising the rate — the conventional cost of disinflation. A cost-push shock confronts the central bank with the output-inflation trade-off: inflation rises while output falls. A demand shock raises output, inflation and the policy rate together. These are theory-consistent benchmarks against which the empirical impulse responses can be assessed.

Input-output propagation

Where the DSGE captures aggregate dynamics, the input-output block captures the structural linkages between sectors. Output is determined by final demand through the Leontief relation, $X=(I-A)^{-1}F$, where $A$ is the matrix of inter-industry technical coefficients and $(I-A)^{-1}$ is the Leontief inverse, whose columns sum to the output multipliers.

The computable general-equilibrium model below extends this with prices, factor markets and substitution.

Computable general equilibrium

The input-output block holds prices and technology fixed. A computable general-equilibrium (CGE) model lifts both: producers and a representative household optimise, all prices and factor returns are determined in equilibrium, and resources reallocate across sectors. This is a small open economy. The oil good is exported at an exogenous world price — so an oil-price fall is a genuine terms-of-trade shock — while the non-oil good is non-traded, its price clearing the domestic market. Labour and capital are mobile with fixed national endowments. Competitive firms price at unit cost, and the two zero-profit conditions pin the wage and rental:

$$ p^{o}=c_{o}(w,r),\quad P_{n}=c_{n}(w,r),\qquad c_{j}(w,r)=\tfrac{1}{\theta_j}\big(\tfrac{w}{a_j}\big)^{a_j}\big(\tfrac{r}{1-a_j}\big)^{1-a_j} $$(C1)

where po is the world oil price (exogenous), Pn the non-oil price, w the wage, r the rental and aj the labour cost share. Given the two prices these equations determine w and r (Stolper–Samuelson); factor-market clearing then sets the output mix (Rybczynski). The household allocates spending between the non-oil good (bearing VAT at rate τ) and imports through a constant-elasticity (Armington, σ = 2) aggregator, so the tax base responds to the rate:

$$ R^{vat}=\tau\,P_n\,C_n,\qquad C_n=s_n\!\big(P_n(1{+}\tau)\big)\cdot\frac{E}{P_n(1{+}\tau)} $$(C2)

where sn is the CES expenditure share on the non-oil good and E total consumer expenditure. The model is calibrated to a benchmark it reproduces exactly (maximum excess demand 1e-16; oil 37 per cent of GDP), then solved under each counterfactual by Newton's method. Welfare is the equivalent variation from the CES expenditure function.

The oil-price shock. Because factors are mobile and fully employed, a thirty per cent fall in the oil price barely changes the quantity of output (-0.1 per cent): oil output falls 18 per cent while non-oil output rises +11 per cent, with about 5 units of labour moving from oil into the non-traded sector. The cost falls instead on real income: with the export good worth thirty per cent less, import capacity contracts, real income drops 9 per cent and welfare about 12 per cent. Real GDP roughly flat but welfare sharply lower is the correct anatomy of a terms-of-trade shock — and the distinction a general-equilibrium model exists to draw.

The VAT cut, and why revenue does not fall in proportion. Cutting the rate from 18 to 15 per cent is a 16.7 per cent reduction; a mechanical rate × base calculation would lose the same 16.7 per cent of revenue. But the cut lowers the consumer price of the non-oil good, and households substitute towards it, so the tax base expands +2.0 per cent. Revenue therefore falls only 15.0 per cent — the difference between the 16.7 per cent rate cut and the 15.0 per cent revenue loss is exactly that behavioural base response, governed by the Armington elasticity. (Under a unit-elastic consumer the base would not move and revenue would fall almost in lock-step with the rate — which is why that specification was replaced.) This is the general-equilibrium counterpart to the simulator's VAT lever.
