Structural Models

The econometric models describe the data; the structural models explain it. A micro-founded New-Keynesian DSGE and an input-output block provide theory-consistent cross-checks and support the policy counterfactuals that reduced-form models cannot address.

Why a structural model

Reduced-form regressions and vector autoregressions summarise historical correlations, but they are silent on the effect of a policy that has no historical precedent, and they are vulnerable to the Lucas critique. A dynamic stochastic general equilibrium (DSGE) model is built from the optimising behaviour of households and firms, so its parameters are structural and its responses can be interpreted as causal under the stated assumptions. The canonical three-equation New-Keynesian model (Galí, 2008) is the standard such framework and the natural complement to the empirical work here.

A three-equation New-Keynesian model

The model comprises a forward-looking investment-saving relation (from the household's consumption-saving choice), a New-Keynesian Phillips curve (from staggered price-setting), and a Taylor rule for monetary policy. It is solved under rational expectations by the method of undetermined coefficients.

$$ \tilde y_t = \mathbb{E}_t \tilde y_{t+1} - \tfrac{1}{\sigma}\big(i_t-\mathbb{E}_t\pi_{t+1}-r^n_t\big)+\varepsilon^d_t $$(D1)
$$ \pi_t=\beta\,\mathbb{E}_t\pi_{t+1}+\kappa\,\tilde y_t+\varepsilon^s_t $$(D2)
$$ i_t=\phi_\pi\,\pi_t+\phi_y\,\tilde y_t+\varepsilon^m_t $$(D3)

Calibrated at conventional values for a small economy: sigma = 1.0, beta = 0.99, kappa = 0.13, phi_pi = 1.5, phi_y = 0.125, rho = 0.5. The discount factor and the inflation-response coefficient follow the standard literature; the slope of the Phillips curve and the interest-elasticity are set to the mid-range of published estimates.

The responses are correctly signed and economically interpretable. A monetary tightening lowers output and inflation while raising the rate — the conventional cost of disinflation. A cost-push shock confronts the central bank with the output-inflation trade-off: inflation rises while output falls. A demand shock raises output, inflation and the policy rate together. These are theory-consistent benchmarks against which the empirical impulse responses can be assessed.

Input-output propagation

Where the DSGE captures aggregate dynamics, the input-output block captures the structural linkages between sectors. Output is determined by final demand through the Leontief relation, $X=(I-A)^{-1}F$, where $A$ is the matrix of inter-industry technical coefficients and $(I-A)^{-1}$ is the Leontief inverse, whose columns sum to the output multipliers.

A full computable general equilibrium model would extend this with prices, factor markets and substitution, and is the natural vehicle for analysing the structural transition away from oil.
