Structural Models

Two structural models complement the reduced-form econometrics: a micro-founded New-Keynesian DSGE and an input-output block. Both are solved and produce impulse responses, providing theory-consistent cross-checks on the empirical forecast.

A three-equation New-Keynesian model

The model comprises a forward-looking investment-saving relation, a New-Keynesian Phillips curve and a Taylor rule, solved under rational expectations by the method of undetermined coefficients.

$$ \tilde y_t = \mathbb{E}_t \tilde y_{t+1} - \tfrac{1}{\sigma}\big(i_t-\mathbb{E}_t\pi_{t+1}-r^n_t\big)+\varepsilon^d_t $$(D1)
$$ \pi_t=\beta\,\mathbb{E}_t\pi_{t+1}+\kappa\,\tilde y_t+\varepsilon^s_t $$(D2)
$$ i_t=\phi_\pi\,\pi_t+\phi_y\,\tilde y_t+\varepsilon^m_t $$(D3)

Calibration: sigma = 1.0, beta = 0.99, kappa = 0.13, phi_pi = 1.5, phi_y = 0.125, rho = 0.5.

Figure 13.1. Responses to a monetary tightening (one hundred basis points): output, inflation and the policy rate.
Figure 13.2. Responses to a cost-push shock, illustrating the output-inflation trade-off.

The responses are correctly signed: a monetary tightening reduces both output and inflation while raising the rate, and a cost-push shock raises inflation while lowering output. This structural layer permits interpretation of the mechanisms behind the empirical results and the analysis of policy counterfactuals that reduced-form models cannot address.

Input-output propagation

A Leontief block, $X=(I-A)^{-1}F$, captures inter-sectoral propagation between the oil and non-oil economies, the basis for a computable general equilibrium analysis of structural shocks.

A ten-unit contraction in oil final demand reduces total output by 14.69 units once inter-industry linkages are accounted for; the multiplier exceeds the direct effect because the non-oil sectors supply inputs to the oil sector. A full computable general equilibrium model would elaborate this channel with prices and factor markets.
