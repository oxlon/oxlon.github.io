Bayesian and State-Space Models

The work programme names a set of models beyond the classical core: a seasonal ARIMA, Bayesian and large Bayesian vector autoregressions, and the Kalman filter. Each is estimated here on the available data and reported with its forecast and an out-of-sample or model-based assessment, so that it is a working result rather than a name on a list.

Seasonal ARIMA

Monthly inflation carries a within-year seasonal pattern that an annual model cannot see. The seasonal ARIMA augments the non-seasonal autoregressive and moving-average polynomials with seasonal counterparts at the twelve-month period:

$$ \Phi_P(L^{12})\,\phi_p(L)\,(1-L)^d(1-L^{12})^D\,y_t=\Theta_Q(L^{12})\,\theta_q(L)\,\varepsilon_t $$(S1)

where yt is monthly inflation, L the lag operator, φ and θ the non-seasonal autoregressive and moving-average polynomials, Φ and Θ their seasonal counterparts at the twelve-month period, and d, D the orders of ordinary and seasonal differencing. The order (1,0,1)(1,0,1,12)12 was selected by the Akaike criterion.

Bayesian VAR with a Minnesota prior

On a short sample a vector autoregression has too many free coefficients to estimate precisely. The Bayesian VAR disciplines them with the Minnesota prior (Litterman, 1986), which shrinks each equation towards a random walk and pulls distant lags and cross-variable terms towards zero. The model is the usual reduced-form VAR,

$$ y_t=c+\sum_{\ell=1}^{p}A_\ell\,y_{t-\ell}+\varepsilon_t,\qquad \varepsilon_t\sim\mathcal N(0,\Sigma) $$(B1)

with the prior placing tighter variance on coefficients that the data are least able to identify:

$$ \mathrm{Var}\!\big(A_\ell^{ij}\big)=\begin{cases}(\lambda_1/\ell)^2 & i=j\\[3pt](\lambda_1\lambda_2/\ell)^2\,\sigma_i^2/\sigma_j^2 & i\neq j\end{cases} $$(B2)

where Aℓ is the coefficient matrix at lag ℓ, λ1 the overall tightness, λ2 the cross-variable shrinkage, and σi, σj residual scales; the posterior is the standard conjugate update. Bands below are 68 and 90 per cent posterior credible intervals from the posterior draws.

Large Bayesian VAR

The same prior makes high-dimensional systems estimable. The large Bayesian VAR here is a six-variable monthly system — inflation, money (M1 and M2 growth), the policy and lending rates, and the oil price — with two lags, so 78 coefficients on 276 observations. Ordinary least squares would be hopeless; the Minnesota prior, by shrinking towards parsimony, delivers a coherent joint forecast (Bańbura, Giannone and Reichlin, 2010). This is the natural route to the larger monitoring systems the work programme envisages.

The Kalman filter — unobserved components

Potential output and the output gap are not observed; they are latent states to be inferred. The unobserved-components model writes output as the sum of a stochastic trend (potential), a cyclical component (the gap) and noise, and the Kalman filter and smoother recover them optimally:

$$ y_t=\mu_t+\psi_t+\varepsilon_t,\qquad \mu_t=\mu_{t-1}+\beta_{t-1}+\eta_t,\quad \beta_t=\beta_{t-1}+\zeta_t $$(K1)
$$ \psi_t=\rho\big(\cos\lambda\,\psi_{t-1}+\sin\lambda\,\psi^{*}_{t-1}\big)+\kappa_t $$(K2)

where μt is the stochastic trend (potential output) with drift βt, ψt the stochastic cycle (the output gap) with frequency λ and damping ρ, and η, ζ, κ, ε mutually independent disturbances. The states are estimated by maximum likelihood through the Kalman filter; the band is ±1 smoothed standard error.

Figure 17.4. Output gap for the non-oil economy: the Kalman-filter (unobserved-components) estimate with its uncertainty band, against the Hodrick–Prescott filter. The two filters agree on the cyclical turning points; the state-space estimate is model-based and carries explicit uncertainty.

The state-space estimate is the model-based complement to the Hodrick–Prescott filter used on the Output page: it imposes an explicit trend-and-cycle structure, returns confidence bands rather than a point line, and extends naturally to nowcasting from mixed-frequency data.
