Mandate and Modelling Framework

The work has two foundations: a statutory requirement, which defines the outputs and their timing, and the Ministry's terms of reference, which define the modelling scope and the deliverables. Both are set out below.

A. Statutory basis

Cabinet of Ministers Decree No. 75 of 24 May 2004, the Rules for the Preparation and Execution of the State Budget, issued under the Budget System Law and Presidential Decree No. 809, governs the budget cycle. The original 2004 text and the consolidated version incorporating the 2023 and 2026 amendments (clauses 3.7-1, 4.2-1, 5.21-1, 5.44 and 5.45) have both been examined.

The forecast initiates the budget cycle
Under clauses 2.2, 2.3 and 2.22, the Ministry of Economy submits a medium-term macroeconomic forecast — the next budget year and the three subsequent years — by 1 March, revised by 1 August. This forecast is the statutory input to the entire budget process.

Indicators required under clause 2.4.1

The Decree enumerates the indicators to be forecast, in three scenarios (baseline, pessimistic and optimistic). Each is produced as a named output of the system.

The fiscal rule and the legal calendar

The Decree requires the ceiling on consolidated-budget expenditure to be computed under the fiscal rule, calculated in accordance with Article 11-1 of the Budget System Law. In force since 2019, the rule combines a ceiling on the use of oil revenue, derived from net financial assets, with a constraint on the ratio of the non-oil base deficit to non-oil output. The statutory deadlines — the March and August forecasts, the July revenue position, the September draft and presidential annex, and the monthly and quarterly execution reports — determine the system's update cadence: monthly during execution, quarterly for re-forecasting, and an annual re-estimation ahead of the September submission.

B. The Ministry's terms of reference

The work programme prepared by the Macroeconomic Analysis and Forecasting Department, under Decree No. 75, defines the scope of the engagement. It is organised around ten forecast directions, a specified set of models, four cross-cutting capabilities, and a work estimate by block. This system has been constructed to address that programme; the report is structured accordingly, and the Modelling Framework records the correspondence between the requested models and the delivered components.

The specified model set

The terms of reference name the methods to be implemented: the classical time-series and structural econometric models — ARIMA and SARIMA, vector autoregressions (including Bayesian, large Bayesian and structural variants), the vector error-correction model, the Hodrick-Prescott and Kalman filters, the input-output model, and the computable general equilibrium and dynamic stochastic general equilibrium models — together with the machine-learning methods specified for the analytics component: regularised regression, random forests, gradient boosting, and the long short-term memory and temporal-fusion neural networks.

Cross-cutting capabilities

Scenario simulation

A what-if engine for shocks such as a fall in the oil price to USD 50, a rise in the policy rate, a reduction in subsidies, or a global recession, with sensitivity matrices, shock propagation and policy-response simulation.

Risk intelligence

Macroeconomic risk heat-maps and early-warning indicators covering fiscal stress, inflation pressure and external imbalance.

Decision support

Executive dashboards for the Ministry's leadership: indicator tracking, forecast comparison and risk visualisation.

Data harmonisation

Cleaning of the historical series, reconciliation of differing methodologies, indicator passports, metadata, and revision management.

Work programme

The terms of reference request an estimate of effort, in person-days, for each block: output, inflation, fiscal, external trade, labour, oil and gas, scenarios, risk, dashboards and harmonisation. This estimate is provided in the Delivery and Next Steps section.

C. Modelling framework

The framework produces the ten forecast directions and four capabilities above using the specified model set, within an internally consistent national-accounts framework that follows the design of the Ministry's Financial-Programming model. The two tables below record the correspondence between what the terms of reference requested and what is ready in the system.

Allocation of methods
The annual statutory projection rests on transparent econometric relationships whose coefficients can be audited before the National Assembly and the Chamber of Accounts. The machine-learning and deep-learning methods are most effective at monthly and transaction-level frequencies, where the evidence in this report shows they add forecasting power, and are used accordingly. The accounting identities that tie the directions together are set out, with the estimated equations they constrain, in the Econometric Models section.
