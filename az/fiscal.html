Fiscal and Budget

The fiscal position is strong but oil-dependent: a surplus and low public debt today, narrowing as oil revenue declines. This section presents the fiscal and external balances, the revenue architecture, and the legal constraints the budget module enforces.

The fiscal and debt position

The consolidated balance is in surplus — +2.6 per cent of output in 2025 on the IMF estimate — but the surplus narrows to -0.6 per cent by 2030 as oil revenue falls. Public debt is low and stable at about 20 per cent of output, which affords the State considerable fiscal space. The external current account remains in surplus throughout, though it too narrows.

Revenue architecture

Each revenue head is modelled on its own base, net of refunds in accordance with clause 2.10.6-1, and aggregated. Customs duties and the transfer from the State Oil Fund are the oil-linked components:

$$ R^{cust}_t=M_t\,\tau^{cust}_t\,e_t,\qquad T^{j}_t=B^{j}_t\,\tau^{j}_t\;\;(j\in\{VAT,CIT,PIT,excise,\dots\}) $$(F1)

The fiscal rule

The expenditure ceiling is defined in Article 11-1 of the Budget System Law. Pending receipt of the implementing regulation, the working specification is

$$ FRC^{ceil}_t=\min\Big(\text{ceiling path}_t,\;\;\text{permitted oil use}(NFA_t)+R^{nonoil}_t\Big) $$(F2)
$$ \text{subject to}\quad \frac{\text{non-oil base deficit}_t}{\text{non-oil GDP}_t} < \frac{\text{non-oil base deficit}_{t-1}}{\text{non-oil GDP}_{t-1}} $$(F3)

This is implemented as an explicit constraint, evaluated on each run, that reports any breach with the corresponding clause reference. The sequestration thresholds of clause 6.3-1 — receipts below target by 5 or 10 per cent — govern the execution-stage monitor. The consolidated deficit excluding the State Oil Fund is the primary anchor under clause 3.4.2.17; the transfer ratio is set by decision rather than formula, and the resulting approximation is documented in the Limitations section.

The central fiscal risk
The surplus and the external position rest on oil revenue. The Scenario Analysis shows that a sustained fall in the oil price erodes both quickly; the fiscal rule, by capping the use of oil revenue and anchoring the non-oil deficit, is the instrument that protects the position. Completing the budget-execution module requires the disaggregated revenue and expenditure data set out in the data request.
