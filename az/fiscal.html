Fiscal and Budget

Azerbaijan's public finances are strong but oil-dependent. This section forecasts the general-government budget — revenue, expenditure, the overall balance and public debt — to 2030, using the time-series and machine-learning models prescribed by the budget methodology, and projects the debt under the standard dynamics. Every projection is conditional on an explicit oil-price and growth scenario.

On the fiscal data
The aggregates are the IMF World Economic Outlook general-government accounts (per cent of GDP, 1994–2025), the internationally standard and internally consistent fiscal dataset. The Ministry's table 2.4.1 Gəlirlər/Xərclər record household money incomes (52–77 per cent of GDP), not budget aggregates, and are not used here. Disaggregated revenue by head — tax, customs and the State Oil Fund transfer — is Ministry-held and is listed in the data request; the forecasting here is at the general-government aggregate. The verified 2025 outturn for the narrower state budget — a surplus of AZN 527.6 million on revenue of AZN 39.1 billion (State Statistical Committee) — is reported on the Budget page; on the general-government basis used here, 2025 is the latest verified outturn.

The fiscal and debt position

The general-government balance is in surplus — +2.6 per cent of output in 2025, the latest outturn — and gross public debt is low and stable at about 20 per cent of output, which affords the State considerable fiscal space. Figure 07.1 distinguishes surplus years (teal) from deficit years (red): the budget has been in surplus throughout the recent period, but the surplus is built on oil revenue and is sensitive to the oil price, as the forecasting below makes precise.

How the budget is forecast

The budget methodology requires the medium-term aggregates to be projected with time-series models — ARIMA and exponential smoothing for the univariate dynamics, error-correction (ARDL) equations for the structural relationships, and vector-autoregressive and vector error-correction systems for the joint dynamics — and with machine-learning models, and the public debt to be projected by the standard debt-dynamics identity. All of these are estimated here. Because revenue is dominated by oil, the forecast is conditional: it takes the oil-price and growth path from the macroeconomic scenario and projects the fiscal aggregates given that path, which is how an oil exporter's budget is properly forecast.

Revenue and expenditure are each modelled as an error-correction (partial-adjustment) equation. Revenue responds to the oil price and the cycle; expenditure is smoother and adjusts towards its own path:

$$ R_t = c + \beta\,\ln(\text{oil}_t) + \gamma\,g_t + \rho\,R_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t $$(F4)
$$ G_t = c + \rho\,G_{t-1} + \gamma\,g_t + \varepsilon_t $$(F5)

where Rt and Gt are general-government revenue and expenditure (per cent of GDP), oilt the average annual Brent price, gt real GDP growth, ρ the persistence (partial-adjustment) coefficient, ln the natural logarithm and εt the disturbance. The long-run response is the impact coefficient divided by (1 − ρ).

Estimated revenue and expenditure equations

The estimates are economically sensible. Revenue rises with the oil price — a 10 per cent higher oil price lifts revenue by about 1.1 percentage points of GDP in the long run — and expenditure is highly persistent, consistent with the smoothing of spending across the cycle. The expenditure equation fits closely (R² = 0.80); revenue is noisier (R² = 0.64) because oil-related receipts are volatile.

Which model forecasts best — the out-of-sample competition

To choose the forecasting model objectively, every candidate is run through an expanding-window backtest: at each year from 2012 the model is estimated only on earlier data and made to predict the next year's balance, given that year's oil price and growth. The 13 one-year-ahead errors are compared against a random walk (last year's balance carried forward). Skill is the percentage reduction in root-mean-square error relative to that benchmark.

For the balance a cluster of regularised and boosting models leads: LASSO (L1), with Elastic Net, HistGradientBoosting and SVR (RBF) essentially tied, each cutting the root-mean-square error to about 5.18 percentage points against 6.54 for the random walk, a 21 per cent improvement, with the oil price the most informative predictor. The margins between the leading models are small and the ranking is not robust on only 13 test years, so the central forecast below uses the structural revenue and expenditure equations above, conditional on the oil-price and growth path, rather than any single competition winner. The ranking is also specific to this target: the flexible neural network that wins the inflation competition over-fits the smoother, more persistent balance and finishes near the bottom here, while the regularised linear models that over-fit inflation do comparatively well. That target-dependence is why the system keeps a panel of models and selects per series rather than committing to one. Public debt dynamics

Gross debt is projected by the standard accumulation identity (budget-methodology Annex 5):

$$ d_t = d_{t-1}\,\frac{1+i_t}{1+g^{n}_t} - pb_t $$(F6)

where d is the gross-debt ratio, i the effective interest rate (calibrated at 4 per cent), gn nominal GDP growth from the output engine, and pb the primary balance (per cent of GDP). The central path follows the WEO baseline — gross debt stays near 20 per cent, because consolidated surpluses accrue to the State Oil Fund rather than retiring gross debt — and the fan widens with the balance's uncertainty (a worse balance must be financed and raises debt).

Figure 07.5. Gross public debt forecast with fan, per cent of output. The central path follows the WEO baseline; the fan is driven by the uncertainty in the balance fed through the debt-dynamics identity.

The long-run sustainability of the position can be tested by asking whether revenue and expenditure share a common trend (a solvency, or no-Ponzi, condition). The Johansen trace statistic is 14.9 against a 5 per cent critical value of 15.5, so the two fall just short of formal cointegration; the vector error-correction estimate nonetheless shows expenditure tracking revenue almost one-for-one in the long run (β ≈ 0.98) with a strong correction of about 51 per cent of any gap each year. The reading is that spending broadly follows revenue, but oil-driven revenue swings are only partly accommodated — which is exactly the risk the fiscal rule is designed to contain.

Revenue architecture and the fiscal rule

Beneath the aggregate forecast, each revenue head is modelled on its own tax base, net of refunds in accordance with clause 2.10.6-1, and the heads are aggregated. Customs duties depend on imports and the exchange rate; each domestic tax is its base times an effective rate:

$$ R^{cust}_t=M_t\,\tau^{cust}_t\,e_t,\qquad T^{j}_t=B^{j}_t\,\tau^{j}_t $$(F1)

where Rcust is customs revenue, M the value of imports, τcust the effective duty rate and e the exchange rate; and, for each domestic tax head j — the value-added tax, corporate income tax, personal income tax, excise and the simplified tax — Tj is the yield, Bj the base and τj the effective rate (the statutory rate adjusted for exemptions). These heads require the disaggregated data listed in the data request; until it is transferred, the forecast operates at the general-government aggregate above.

The expenditure ceiling is defined in Article 11-1 of the Budget System Law. Pending the implementing regulation, the working specification caps consolidated expenditure at the lower of a permitted growth path and the sum of a sustainable use of oil revenue and of non-oil revenue:

$$ FRC^{ceil}_t=\min\Big(\text{ceiling path}_t,\;\;\text{permitted oil use}(NFA_t)+R^{nonoil}_t\Big) $$(F2)
$$ \text{subject to}\quad \frac{\text{non-oil base deficit}_t}{\text{non-oil GDP}_t} < \frac{\text{non-oil base deficit}_{t-1}}{\text{non-oil GDP}_{t-1}} $$(F3)

where FRCceil is the consolidated-expenditure ceiling, NFA the State's net financial assets — which set the sustainable use of oil revenue — and Rnonoil non-oil revenue; the constraint requires the non-oil base deficit, as a share of non-oil output, to fall year on year. It is implemented as an explicit constraint, evaluated on each run, that reports any breach with the corresponding clause reference; the sequestration thresholds of clause 6.3-1 govern the execution-stage monitor.

The central fiscal risk
The surplus and the debt position rest on oil revenue. The scenarios show that a sustained fall in the oil price erases the surplus and lifts debt within a few years; the fiscal rule, by capping the use of oil revenue and anchoring the non-oil deficit, is the instrument that protects the position. Completing the head-level revenue and expenditure modules requires the disaggregated data set out in the data request.
