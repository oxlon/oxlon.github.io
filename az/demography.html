The demographic structure

Azerbaijan's population is still growing but ageing and urbanising. On World Bank data the country is about 59 per cent urban, and the age-dependency ratio, the young and old relative to the working-age population, stands near 44 per cent, a comparatively favourable demographic window that is slowly closing as growth slows. These structural ratios frame the level projection that follows.

Figure 11.1. Demographic structure: urbanisation rate and age-dependency ratio, per cent, 2000–2024. Source: World Bank World Development Indicators.

Population

Population growth has slowed from above one per cent to about 0.4 per cent and is projected to keep easing to roughly 0.1 per cent by 2030, reaching about 10.3 million on the Oxlon path, a little below the Ministry's figure. The slowing denominator means GDP per capita rises faster than output: from 12.6 to 16.1 thousand manat.

The projection is parametric. The annual growth rate is fitted as a linearly declining trend in time, floored at a small positive value so the population keeps rising but ever more slowly, and the level is compounded forward:

$$ g_t=\max\!\big(a-b\,(t-2013),\ \underline{g}\,\big),\qquad N_t=N_{t-1}\,(1+g_t) $$(D1)

where Nt is the mid-year population, gt the implied growth rate, a and b the intercept and slope of the declining-growth trend estimated from 2013 onward, and g with an underbar a small positive floor that keeps growth from turning negative within the horizon.

Consumption

Household expenditure is modelled on household income in a log-difference, constant-elasticity form, so the growth of consumption moves with the growth of income up to an estimated elasticity:

$$ \Delta\log(C_t)=\alpha+\epsilon\,\Delta\log(Y_t)+u_t $$(D2)

where Ct is household consumption expenditure, Yt household income, α a constant drift and ε the income elasticity of consumption. The estimate ε ≈ 0.88 (R² = 0.63) sits just under one, implying a little is saved out of each additional manat of income, a modest but non-trivial saving rate at the margin.

On this relation, household expenditure expands broadly in step with incomes across the horizon. Paid services to the population grow faster than expenditure as a whole, in line with rising incomes and a gradual shift towards services. The Oxlon household-consumption fan in Figure 11.4 carries this central path forward with explicit uncertainty, setting the 2025 jump-off against 50 and 80 per cent probability bands.

The reading

The pieces fit into one coherent picture. Population growth slows from about 0.4 per cent now to roughly 0.1 per cent by 2030, close to flat, taking the headcount to about 10.3 million, a touch below the Ministry's projection. That slowing denominator is what lifts GDP per capita from 12.6 to about 16.1 thousand manat over the horizon, faster than output itself. Household consumption, governed by the near-unit income elasticity, rises broadly with income, with paid services leading the way.

Critical evaluation
Three qualifications bound this section. The population path is a simple trend extrapolation of the growth rate, not a cohort-component projection: it carries no explicit fertility, mortality or migration structure, so it should be read as indicative of direction rather than as a demographic forecast. The household income and household expenditure series are money flows of the population and are distinct from national-accounts private consumption, so the consumption fan should not be read against the GDP expenditure aggregate. And the per-capita figures for 2025–2030 divide output by the Ministry's population denominator rather than the Oxlon one, which is marginally higher; using the Oxlon lower headcount would lift per-capita income a little further. The block is best read as a consistent demographic and consumption direction, not a point prediction.
