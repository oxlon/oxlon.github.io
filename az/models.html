Econometric Models and Results

This section sets out the variables and their construction, the form of each model block, the estimated equation as deployed, and the result that follows it. The deployed system comprises ninety-two equations; the representative relationship for each block is shown with its estimated coefficients, and the inflation equation, which is re-estimated here, is reported in full with its specification table, robustness checks and fit.

National accounts

The forecast directions are tied together by the national-accounts identities, which are enforced as explicit checks rather than assumed. Output is the sum of oil and non-oil value added, and headline real growth is the previous year's nominal shares applied to the two sectoral growth rates:

$$ GDP_t = VA^{oil}_t + VA^{nonoil}_t,\qquad \hat g^{GDP}_t=\omega^{oil}_{t-1}\,\hat g^{oil}_t+\omega^{nonoil}_{t-1}\,\hat g^{nonoil}_t $$(N1)

where VA is sector value added, ĝ the real growth rate and ω the nominal output share. Both identities were verified against the Ministry's data to within 0.05 per cent, which confirms the directions reconcile to one consistent national-accounts framework. The behavioural equations below are estimated subject to these identities.

The real expenditure components combine short-run dynamics with convergence to a long-run equilibrium, in the error-correction form that has been the workhorse of applied macro-modelling since cointegration was formalised (Engle and Granger, 1987; Pesaran, Shin and Smith, 2001). Private consumption, for example, is

$$ \Delta\log C_t=\alpha\,\Delta\log Y^d_t-\gamma\,\hat r^{L}_t-\lambda\,(\log C_{t-1}-\beta\log Y^d_{t-1})+\varepsilon_t $$(M1)

where C is real household consumption, Yd disposable income, r̂L the real lending-rate gap and ε the disturbance; the term in brackets is the deviation from the long-run consumption–income relationship, which the model corrects at speed λ. The coefficients α, γ, β are short-run elasticities.

The deployed equations have exactly this form: consumption responds to real labour income and corrects towards its long-run level, while investment responds to the oil-price index, domestic demand and real money — reflecting the dependence of capital formation on oil revenue.

Inflation and monetary

Consumer-price inflation is modelled as an open-economy Phillips curve, the standard New-Keynesian relationship augmented with imported prices (Galí and Gertler, 1999):

$$ \pi_t=\phi\,\pi_{t-1}+(1-\phi)\,\pi^{e}_t+\kappa\,\hat y_t+\theta\big(\Delta s_t+\Delta p^{*}_t\big)+\varepsilon_t $$(M2)

where π is inflation, πe expected inflation, ŷ the output gap, Δs the change in the exchange rate and Δp* world (import) price inflation; φ is the weight on past inflation, κ the slope of the curve and θ the pass-through coefficient.

Persistence alone (column 1) explains only about a sixth of the variation. Adding the exchange rate (column 2) adds little, but adding the world food price (column 3) raises the fit to about sixty-three per cent, which identifies imported prices, and food in particular, as the dominant proximate driver of Azerbaijani inflation. Once the exchange rate and food prices are present, the oil price (column 4) and broad money (column 5) add essentially nothing, so they work through those channels rather than directly. The exchange-rate coefficient is stable at about 0.20 to 0.22 across columns (3) to (5).

The contemporaneous exchange rate is plausibly determined jointly with inflation, which would bias its coefficient. Replacing it with its lagged value lowers the estimate to about 0.10, so part of the contemporaneous coefficient reflects simultaneity, and the causal pass-through is best read as a range of roughly 0.10 to 0.21. The oil price, by contrast, is exogenous to a price-taking economy and serves as a clean external regressor. The relationship strengthens in the post-2008 sub-sample (R² of about 0.72) and is unaffected by Newey–West standard errors, so the result is not driven by the early-transition years or by residual serial correlation.

One caveat carries into the forecast: in-sample fit overstates forecasting accuracy. The out-of-sample comparison in the Machine-Learning section shows the multivariate linear specification does not forecast well on this short sample, which is why the headline projection rests on a combination of models and on the monthly panel rather than on this single equation.

Labour and social

Wages follow a Phillips relationship in productivity and prices, and Okun's law (1962) links unemployment to the non-oil output gap:

$$ \Delta W_t=\delta+\zeta\,\Delta(\text{productivity})_t-\eta\,\pi_{t-1}+\varepsilon_t $$(M3)

where W is the average wage (estimated separately for each activity), productivity is sectoral labour productivity, and πt−1 is lagged inflation; ζ and η are the productivity and price elasticities.

Oil revenue and the sovereign fund

The transfer from the State Oil Fund — the single largest revenue line — is an identity in production, price and the government's share, with a behavioural transfer ratio:

$$ T^{SOFAZ}_t=q^{oil}_t\big(p^{oil}_t e_t-c\big)\,\sigma^{gov}\,\kappa_t,\qquad \kappa_t=f(\text{non-oil deficit gap}_{t-1}) $$(M4)

where TSOFAZ is the transfer, qoil oil-and-gas production, poil the world price, e the AZN/USD rate, c the unit extraction cost, σgov the government's production share and κ the transfer ratio to the budget.

Production is exogenous, following production-sharing-agreement profiles; the transfer ratio κ responds to the prior-year non-oil deficit — a political-economy regularity. The dependence on this discretionary parameter is discussed in the Limitations section.
