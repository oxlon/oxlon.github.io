Machine-Learning Models

The terms of reference specify a wide set of machine-learning methods. All were implemented and evaluated against classical benchmarks under out-of-sample testing, so that their value is judged on evidence rather than asserted. This section reports the comparison on annual inflation and a deep-learning model on the monthly series.

Methodology and rationale

Time-series models must be evaluated out of sample and in chronological order: each is estimated on data up to a given year and asked to predict the next, the window expanding as it rolls forward. This walk-forward protocol avoids the look-ahead bias that in-sample fit conceals. The model set deliberately spans the bias–variance spectrum — from parsimonious linear and univariate models, through regularised regressions, to flexible tree ensembles and neural networks — because on a short, noisy annual sample the more flexible models are prone to overfit, and only an out-of-sample contest reveals which generalise. Skill is the improvement in root-mean-square error over a random-walk benchmark, the appropriate null for a persistent series.

Annual model comparison

Each model was evaluated by expanding-window, one-step-ahead testing on annual inflation (2000–2024; 13 out-of-sample years). Skill denotes the improvement in root-mean-square error over a random-walk benchmark. The gradient-boosting results use the XGBoost and LightGBM libraries.

Findings on the annual sample

On approximately 25 annual observations, parsimony is decisive. A small neural network and a univariate time-series model generalise best; tree-based ensembles are competitive; multivariate linear regressions with collinear regressors perform well in sample but extrapolate poorly out of sample. The importance ranking corroborates the structural pass-through relationship, placing the world food price, the exchange rate and the oil price foremost.

Deep learning on the monthly series

A long short-term memory network was trained on the monthly panel (1997-01–2024-12; 211 months for training, 96 for testing) to forecast inflation 12 months ahead from a multivariate sequence of inflation, oil-price growth and money growth. At this horizon, persistence is a weak benchmark and the network's use of the additional series is informative.

The network reduces the forecast error by 32% relative to persistence, confirming that the value of these methods rises with data frequency and forecast horizon. The deployment of machine learning is therefore directed to high-frequency forecasting and, prospectively, to the transaction-level applications described in the Delivery section.

Prophet and the Temporal Fusion Transformer

Two further methods named in the work programme are evaluated on the monthly panel. Prophet (Taylor and Letham, 2018) decomposes the series into a piecewise-linear trend with automatic changepoints and a yearly seasonal term; over a twelve-month horizon it forecasts the price level with a mean absolute percentage error of 0.94 per cent against 1.02 for a seasonal-naive benchmark, its value lying as much in the interpretable trend-and-seasonality decomposition as in the headline accuracy.

The Temporal Fusion Transformer (Lim et al., 2019) combines variable selection, an LSTM encoder and interpretable multi-head attention, and emits quantile forecasts. Trained on the monthly panel with the oil-price, money and interest-rate covariates, it forecasts the held-out final year with a root-mean-square error of 0.38 points against 0.76 for persistence — a 50 per cent reduction.
