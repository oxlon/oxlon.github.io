Scenario Analysis

The medium-term outlook is conditioned on assumptions that may not hold. Scenario analysis quantifies the consequences of the principal risks — above all the oil price — for output, inflation and the public finances.

Why scenarios, not a single forecast

No model forecasts the oil price reliably at budget-relevant horizons; the futures curve and survey forecasts are, in practice, little better than a random walk (Kilian, 2009). The disciplined response is not to predict the price but to condition on a small set of plausible paths and report the outcomes of each. Decree No. 75 institutionalises this in requiring three scenarios — baseline, pessimistic and optimistic — and the system reports them together throughout.

The conservative path corresponds to approximately one standard deviation of annual oil-price changes; the stress path to the experience of the 2015–16 collapse.

Figure 12.1 sets out the economy's exposure to the oil price directly. For any assumed price on the horizontal axis, it reads off the resulting deviation — from the USD-65 baseline — of total output and of oil-related fiscal revenue, both zero at the baseline by construction. Two features stand out: the lines steepen on the downside, and oil-related revenue is far more sensitive than output, because the budget depends disproportionately on oil. The three statutory scenarios are not separate calculations but points on this single continuum.

Figure 12.1. Sensitivity of the economy to the oil price. For each assumed oil price (horizontal axis), the lines give the deviation of total output (left axis) and of oil-related fiscal revenue (right axis) from the USD-65 baseline; both are zero at the baseline by construction. The shaded band marks prices below baseline — the downside — and the three statutory scenarios (stress ≈ $45, conservative ≈ $55, baseline $65) are marked.

A comparison of named scenarios

The simulator's transmission channels are applied to a set of policy-relevant scenarios, reporting the 2030 outcome for each. The oil-driven scenarios dominate the fiscal result; a devaluation is principally an inflation event; fiscal consolidation improves the balance at a modest cost to output.
