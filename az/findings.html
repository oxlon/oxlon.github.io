1 · Output: moderate growth and a continuing structural shift

The baseline projects average real output growth of 2.6 per cent over 2026–2030. The composition of output continues to shift towards the non-oil economy, whose share rises from 72 per cent in 2025 to 81 per cent by 2030 as oil and gas production declines in real terms. The reconstructed national-accounts identities reconcile to within 0.05 per cent and the engine reproduces the official projection, establishing that the annual statutory forecast can be operated on the data already held.

2 · Inflation: an external, pass-through process

Consumer-price inflation is determined principally by exchange-rate and import-price pass-through. A one per cent depreciation of the manat raises prices by approximately 0.21 percentage points (coefficient of determination 0.63 over 26 years), and a one-standard-deviation oil-price shock raises inflation by about 2.2 percentage points on impact before dissipating within three years. The baseline path rises from 5.6 per cent in 2025 to 4.7 per cent in 2030. Under the managed exchange-rate regime the parity is the dominant transmission channel, and the exchange rate is accordingly a primary scenario control.

3 · Oil and the fiscal accounts: the principal risk

Oil-price movements are the dominant source of macro-fiscal risk. In the computable general-equilibrium model a thirty per cent fall in the oil price leaves real output little changed in quantity (factors reallocate to the non-oil sector) but cuts real national income by about 9 per cent and household welfare by about 12 per cent through the terms of trade, and it contracts oil-related revenue — including the transfer from the State Oil Fund — substantially. The price is not forecast; it is specified as three scenarios — baseline, conservative and stress — which are reported together throughout.

4 · Forecast performance across the model suite

The econometric models, the machine-learning toolkit (including the gradient-boosting libraries) and a deep-learning network were implemented and compared under out-of-sample testing. On the short annual sample a parsimonious neural network and a univariate time-series model are most accurate, with tree-based ensembles close behind; the leading annual model improves upon a no-change benchmark by 38%. On the monthly series a long short-term memory network forecasts inflation twelve months ahead with a root-mean-square error of 4.547, against 6.678 for the benchmark, a reduction of 32%.

5 · Structural validation

A three-equation New-Keynesian model yields responses consistent with theory: a monetary tightening reduces output by 1.13 per cent and inflation by 0.29 per cent on impact while raising the policy rate. It provides an internally-coherent cross-check on the empirical estimates.

Recommendations

  1. Adopt the consolidated system as the statutory forecasting engine; it reproduces the official projection and is fully documented.
  2. Forecast inflation from a combination of the better-performing models, and apply machine learning at the monthly and transaction-level frequencies, where its advantage is established.
  3. Report all three oil scenarios in budget documentation, in view of the centrality of the oil price to the outlook.
  4. Obtain the quarterly national accounts, the implementing regulation of the fiscal rule, and the disaggregated revenue and expenditure data, in order to complete the quarterly structural model and the budget-execution module.
  5. Establish the production environment: an automated data pipeline, executive reporting, and a programme of staff training.
