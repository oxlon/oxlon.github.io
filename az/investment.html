The accelerator and the oil cycle

Gross fixed capital formation is modelled as an accelerator-plus-oil process: the growth of real investment responds to the pace of real output growth — the accelerator mechanism, under which firms add to the capital stock when demand is expanding — and to the oil cycle, with a lag to capture the gradual adjustment of investment plans. The estimated relationship is:

$$ \Delta\log(I_t)=\alpha+\beta\,g_t+\delta\,\Delta\log(\mathrm{oil}_t)+\rho\,\Delta\log(I_{t-1})+\varepsilon_t $$(I1)

where Δlog(It) is the growth of real fixed investment, gt real GDP growth, Δlog(oilt) the change in the log oil price and Δlog(It−1) lagged investment growth; α is the constant, β the accelerator (estimated at +0.27), δ the oil-price sensitivity (estimated at +0.065), ρ the persistence of investment growth (+0.15) and εt the disturbance. The equation is estimated by ordinary least squares on 2013–2025 actuals, with a coefficient of determination of R² = 0.14.

The baseline and the investment ratio

Both channels carry the expected positive sign — the accelerator at +0.27 per point of output growth and the oil-price sensitivity at +0.065, so a ten per cent rise in the oil price adds roughly 0.7 per cent to investment growth. Figure 9.1 sets the central investment path against 50 and 80 per cent probability bands. Total fixed investment reaches about 25 billion manat by 2030, close to and a little below the Ministry's own projection of 27 billion. The investment-to-GDP ratio (Figure 9.2) eases from about 17.3 per cent in 2025 to roughly 16 per cent by 2030, as nominal output outpaces capital formation across the horizon.

Critical evaluation
The relationship is estimated on only about ten usable annual observations, and on so short a sample none of the coefficients is statistically significant — the accelerator carries a p-value of 0.83, the oil-price term 0.57, the lagged-investment term 0.69 and the constant 0.62 — while the fit is modest (R² = 0.14). The signs are therefore reliable but the magnitudes are no more than indicative. Two further caveats bound the projection. The domestic and foreign split is imposed as a fixed recent-average share rather than modelled, so it cannot shift with the cycle. And foreign investment is lumpy — it is driven by individual oil and gas projects that arrive in discrete steps — and is not well captured by the smooth estimated equation, which is better read as a description of the aggregate trend than of the foreign component.
