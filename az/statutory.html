Statutory Tables in 8 vərəq.xlsx
Independent Reproduction and Comparison

8 vərəq.xlsx is the Ministry's statutory workbook of macroeconomic projections — six sheets, the sub-tables numbered 2.4.1.1 through 2.4.1.14. This page rebuilds those sheets with Oxlon's own forecasting where the data allows, sets each line against the Ministry's Decree-75 figures and the IMF, and is explicit about what it cannot yet forecast. Where Oxlon holds a model for a line, its forecast is shown; every other line is reproduced from Decree-75 or marked as a data gap. The forecasts come from skill-weighted model combinations — and, for headline GDP, the oil/non-oil identity — documented below with the estimated equations and cross-checked against the Ministry's own CAEM engine.

What can be forecast, and what cannot

The workbook holds six sheets. Oxlon's own models independently forecast three lines within it — real GDP growth, non-oil growth and consumer-price inflation, all in sheet 2.4.1.1 — and every other line is reproduced from the Ministry's Decree-75 figures. Three sheets (2.4.1.1, 2.4.1.5–7, 2.4.1.13–14) can be reproduced in full; the other three need series Oxlon does not yet hold before they could be forecast at all. The data needed to close those gaps is set out in the request at the foot of this page.

Oxlon's forecast is skill-weighted, not an average

Oxlon's inflation and non-oil-growth projections are not a simple mean of model outputs; headline GDP is then built from the oil/non-oil identity (the non-oil ensemble plus an explicit oil-sector path), with the time-series ensemble on total GDP reported below serving as an independent cross-check. Each candidate model is estimated, then scored by its out-of-sample (OOS) accuracy — its root-mean-square error (RMSE, the typical size of a forecast miss) measured only on years the model was not fitted to, by rolling pseudo-real-time evaluation. The models are combined in proportion to that measured accuracy, with any model that fails to beat a naïve random walk (this year equals last year) receiving no weight. Forecast combination of this kind has been shown to dominate any single model in expectation (Bates and Granger, 1969; Timmermann, 2006), because it diversifies away model-specific error. The candidate set spans five complementary representations of the same series:

$$ \textbf{AR(1):}\quad y_t = c + \rho\,y_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t $$(E1)
$$ \textbf{ECM:}\quad \Delta y_t = \alpha\,\Delta x_t - \lambda\big(y_{t-1}-\beta x_{t-1}\big) + \varepsilon_t $$(E2)
$$ \textbf{Potential output:}\quad \hat g_t = \exp\!\big(\tau_t-\tau_{t-1}\big)-1,\qquad \tau=\arg\min_\tau \sum_t (y_t-\tau_t)^2+\lambda\sum_t(\Delta^2\tau_t)^2 $$(E3)
$$ \textbf{VAR}(p)\;/\;\textbf{BVAR:}\quad \mathbf{Y}_t = \nu + \sum_{i=1}^{p}\mathbf{A}_i\,\mathbf{Y}_{t-i} + \boldsymbol\varepsilon_t $$(E4)

where y is the target series (real growth or inflation), x a cointegrating driver, τ the Hodrick–Prescott trend (λ=100 on annual data), and Y the vector of jointly-modelled macro variables; the BVAR adds a Minnesota prior (Litterman, 1986) to control the dimensionality of the short sample. For inflation the set also includes a target/expectations anchor and the structural Phillips curve described below.

The combination assigns each model a weight inversely proportional to its mean-squared forecast error, restricted to the models that are themselves skilful:

$$ w_i=\frac{\text{RMSE}_i^{-2}\,\mathbb{1}\{\text{RMSE}_i<\text{RMSE}_{\text{RW}}\}}{\sum_j \text{RMSE}_j^{-2}\,\mathbb{1}\{\cdot\}},\qquad \hat y^{\,\text{Oxlon}}_t=\sum_i w_i\,\hat y^{(i)}_t $$(E5)

so a model that does not beat the random walk receives zero weight, and the most accurate models dominate the combination. The realised weights are reported below; they are far from equal, which is the whole point — the simple average of the candidate models would be materially less accurate than Oxlon's skill-weighted combination.

For real GDP growth all five models beat the random walk on this sample: the potential-output model is the most accurate single method and earns the largest weight, with the autoregression second. This time-series combination gives growth a little below the identity-based headline (it averages the low-single-digit range), which it corroborates rather than competes with. For inflation the autoregression and the expectations anchor share the weight almost equally, while the exchange-rate pass-through error-correction model and the vector autoregressions are heavily penalised and dropped. Oxlon's combination beats the random-walk benchmark in both cases.

A structural cross-check

The ensemble is cross-checked against a compact structural model — Oxlon's reproduction of the Ministry's own CAEM engine. Two blocks bear on the tables here: the output gap (the deviation of activity from its Hodrick–Prescott trend — a standard statistical filter that separates trend from cycle) and an open-economy Phillips curve for inflation. The output-gap block reproduces the trend non-oil growth published on the Ministry's CAEM “A1” sheet to a mean 0.14 pp over 2014–2029 — its validated strength. Its own 2026 growth, 2.9 per cent, sits a little above the ensemble (2.1) and the IMF (2.2) but in the same low-single-digit range, so it corroborates the ensemble's order of magnitude rather than competing with it as the headline figure.

$$ \hat y_t=\big(\ln Q_t-\tau_t\big)\times100,\qquad \tau=\text{HP}_{\lambda=100}(\ln Q) $$(C1)
$$ \pi_t=c_1\pi_{t-1}+(1-c_1-c_2)\,\mathbb{E}_t\pi+c_2\big(\pi^{m}_t+\Delta e_t-\bar\pi^{m}_t\big)+c_3\,\hat y_t+a_t $$(C2)

with calibration read from the workbook: c1=0.10, c2=0.20, c3=0.40 in the Phillips curve; πm is imported-price inflation and Δe the exchange-rate change. The engine's policy-rate and debt blocks are not used here, because 8 vərəq.xlsx has no monetary or fiscal table to reproduce.

Headline comparison

The three series Oxlon forecasts with an independent model are shown over the forecast horizon 2026–2030 against the Ministry and the IMF, on a scale tight enough to separate the lines (realised years are not plotted — 2025 is the last actual, and forecasting an already-known year would not be meaningful). Two features stand out: Oxlon and the IMF stay close on growth — both in the low-to-mid two-per-cent range, Oxlon a little below the IMF early on and converging by 2030 — while the Ministry's path is stronger and more volatile; and the Ministry's inflation path climbs above seven per cent by 2030, far above Oxlon (which holds near five and a quarter) and the IMF (which settles at four). These differences are reported as they stand.

What could not be calculated, and why

2.4.1.2 production structure and 2.4.1.3 sectoral structure
Oxlon has catalogued the thirty-eight national-accounts equations but not the live per-industry series they require (gross output, intermediate consumption and value added by branch of activity, classified on NACE Rev. 2 — the EU standard industry classification). Until those arrive the sheets are reproduced from Decree-75, not forecast.
2.4.1.4 physical output — the largest gap
The 165 physical-output items (wheat in tonnes, livestock in head, gold in kilograms, manufacturing volumes) have a history but essentially no estimated production models, so they can only be trend-carried — which Oxlon does not present as a forecast.
2.4.1.5–7 and 2.4.1.13–14 are reproduced, not forecast
These sheets are shown in the Ministry's own figures. To forecast them Oxlon needs the sectoral investment, wage and employment series behind 2.4.1.5–7, and the disaggregated trade (oil / non-oil) and the full balance of payments on the IMF's BPM6 standard (the sixth Balance of Payments Manual) behind 2.4.1.13–14 — including the financial account (direct investment, the oil bonus, portfolio and reserve assets).

The complete list of series required to close every gap — with units, frequency, history and source — is set out in the data request at the foot of this page.

Assumptions and provenance

What is computed, and what is hand-set
Every value in the tables and figures on this page is either produced by Oxlon's models or carried from a cited source — none is hand-entered to make a line look right, and the counts and validation figures above are read straight from the data at build time. The few hand-set numbers are assumptions, not results: the Phillips-curve calibration read from the CAEM workbook (c₁=0.10, c₂=0.20, c₃=0.40), and the oil-price and exchange-rate paths noted below. Change any of these and the affected lines recompute.

Conditioning assumptions: oil price n/a (USD/bbl); exchange rate de-facto peg 1.7 AZN/USD. No live WRDS access in this build — the report draws on Oxlon's local data and already-downloaded public sources: the State Statistical Committee / Decree-75 statutory tables; World Bank World Development Indicators for history; FRED for the Brent series; and, as the external benchmark, the IMF. The IMF comparison figures are taken from IMF Country Report No. 26/112 — the Staff Report for the 2026 Article IV Consultation (issued May 2026), table “Selected Economic and Financial Indicators, 2023–31” — and agree with the World Economic Outlook. Nothing is fabricated: every figure traces to one of these sources or to Oxlon's models, and every blank is a genuine data or model gap, documented above.
