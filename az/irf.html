Impulse Responses

How does a shock propagate through the economy over time? This section estimates a structural vector autoregression in three variables — the oil price, the exchange rate and inflation — and reports the full set of dynamic responses and the variance decomposition, with the identifying assumptions stated.

Why a structural vector autoregression

Single equations describe one relationship at a time and cannot trace how a disturbance to one variable works through the others over several years. The vector autoregression (Sims, 1980) treats the variables as a system and, once the reduced-form residuals are mapped to economically-meaningful structural shocks, yields impulse responses — the dynamic effect of a one-off shock — and a variance decomposition — the share of each variable's fluctuations attributable to each shock. For an oil economy the natural questions are precisely of this form: what does an oil-price shock do to the exchange rate and to inflation, and over what horizon.

Model and identification

Let $z_t=(\,\Delta\log p^{oil}_t,\ \Delta\log e_t,\ \pi_t\,)'$ collect oil-price growth, the change in the exchange rate and inflation. The reduced-form model of order one is

$$ z_t = c + A\,z_{t-1} + u_t,\qquad \mathbb{E}[u_t u_t']=\Sigma $$(V1)

Structural shocks $\varepsilon_t$ are identified by a recursive (Cholesky) decomposition $\Sigma=PP'$, with $u_t=P\varepsilon_t$ and $P$ lower-triangular, under the ordering oil price → exchange rate → inflation:

$$ u_t = P\,\varepsilon_t,\qquad P=\begin{bmatrix} p_{11} & 0 & 0 \\ p_{21} & p_{22} & 0 \\ p_{31} & p_{32} & p_{33} \end{bmatrix} $$(V2)

The ordering encodes defensible timing assumptions: the world oil price is not contemporaneously influenced by domestic variables (Azerbaijan is a price-taker); the managed exchange rate adjusts next; and consumer prices respond last. This is the identification of the oil-shock literature (Kilian, 2009). The model is estimated on annual data (2000–2025, 26 observations) and responses are shown with two-standard-error bands.

Headline responses to an oil-price shock

A one-standard-deviation rise in the oil price raises inflation by approximately 2.2 percentage points on impact and is associated with an appreciation of the managed parity; both effects decay within about three years. This is the signature of an economy that imports much of its consumption while exporting oil.

Variance decomposition

The forecast-error variance decomposition reports the share of each variable's fluctuations explained by each shock at the 10-year horizon. Oil-price and exchange-rate shocks together account for about 41 per cent of the variance of inflation, with the remainder its own persistence; the oil shock alone explains roughly 55 per cent of exchange-rate fluctuations. This quantifies the external, oil-driven character of Azerbaijani inflation.

High-frequency cross-check
A monetary vector autoregression on the monthly panel (2005-02–2024-12, 239 observations) confirms the limited monthly pass-through under the managed regime and exhibits the price puzzle common to such systems; this absence of the exchange-rate and activity channels at monthly frequency motivates the quarterly five-variable structural model set out in the Limitations and Delivery sections.
