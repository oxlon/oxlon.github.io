Labour Market

Unemployment, employment and wages. The unemployment rate is forecast from an Okun's-law equation estimated on World Bank data; employment is tied to output through a companion Okun relation; wages are tested against a Phillips relation. The result is a tight market in which moderate growth slowly absorbs the remaining slack.

The labour market in ratios, not levels

Labour-market conditions are read as rates, not head-counts. Azerbaijan's labour-force participation has held near 64 per cent and the employment-to-population ratio near 60 per cent over the past decade, while youth unemployment, at about 14 per cent, runs well above the headline rate. These structural ratios frame the forecast that follows.

Unemployment: an Okun's-law forecast

The headline forecast is the unemployment rate. Okun's law links the change in unemployment to the pace of output growth: when the economy grows faster than its trend, unemployment falls. Estimated in first differences on the World Bank series for 2001-2024 (24 years):

$$ \Delta u_t = \alpha + \beta\, g_t + \varepsilon_t $$(L1)

where Δut is the year-on-year change in the unemployment rate (percentage points), gt real GDP growth, α the constant, β the Okun coefficient and εt the disturbance. The estimate is β = -0.032 (p = 0.022, R² = 0.22): each additional point of growth lowers the unemployment rate by about 0.03 of a percentage point, the correctly-signed and statistically significant relation Okun's law predicts. Rolling the equation forward along the Oxlon real-growth path, from the 5.3 per cent rate of 2025, gives the central projection in Figure 8.2.

Figure 8.2. Unemployment rate: World Bank outturn to 2024 and the Oxlon Okun's-law projection for 2026–2030 (per cent), with 50% and 80% bands and the IMF World Economic Outlook path as an independent comparator.

The Oxlon equation brings the unemployment rate down from 5.3 per cent in 2025 to 4.8 per cent by 2030. The IMF projects 5.0 per cent over the same horizon: the two paths sit within a fifth of a point of each other throughout, an independent cross-check that the labour-market outlook is not an artefact of one method.

Employment: the companion relation

Employment is linked to output by a companion Okun relation in growth rates, so its slope is the employment–output elasticity:

$$ \Delta\log(\mathrm{emp}_t)=a+b\,g_t+\varepsilon_t $$(L2)

with Δlog(empt) the growth of total employment and gt real GDP growth. The elasticity is b = 0.38 (R² = 0.40): about 0.38 of a point of employment growth per point of output growth, the modest pass-through expected where the capital-intensive oil sector adds little to head-count. Applying it to the Oxlon growth path lifts employment to about 5440 thousand persons by 2030; the band widens with the horizon as the regression error compounds.

Wages: a relation that does not hold

Nominal wages are tested against a wage-Phillips relation in growth rates, inflation and a lagged wage-growth term allowing persistence:

$$ \Delta\log(w_t)=c+\varphi\,\pi_t+\rho\,\Delta\log(w_{t-1})+\varepsilon_t $$(L3)

where Δlog(wt) is nominal wage growth, πt inflation and Δlog(wt−1) its lag. The fit is negligible (R² = 0.01, with φ ≈ 0.07 and ρ ≈ -0.05 both near zero). That near-zero fit is itself the finding: Azerbaijani wage growth is set by discrete public-sector pay decisions, not by a market wage-Phillips mechanism, so it is not forecastable from inflation and the projection follows the Ministry's plan. Real wage growth is nonetheless modestly positive, about +2.7 per cent in 2030.

What the forecast says

The picture is a tight, slowly tightening labour market. The unemployment rate eases from 5.3 per cent in 2025 to 4.8 per cent by 2030 on the Okun's-law equation, employment rises to roughly 5440 thousand persons, and real wages grow modestly. With participation already high and little slack to absorb, the gains from further output growth show up more in pay than in large falls in unemployment.

Critical evaluation
Three caveats bound this block. The unemployment equation is a parsimonious Okun's law: it is correctly signed and significant (beta = -0.032, p = 0.022) but explains only part of the variation (R2 = 0.22) on 24 annual observations, so the bands are deliberately wide and the path is conditional on the Oxlon growth forecast. The wage equation does not hold at all (R2 = 0.01): pay is administratively set, so its projection follows the Ministry plan rather than a market mechanism, and this is disclosed rather than hidden. And both Okun relations are conditional on the central growth path, so a growth disappointment feeds directly through to employment and unemployment. The forecast is best read as a consistent central path with appropriate bands, validated against the IMF, not a point prediction.
