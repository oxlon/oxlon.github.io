Labour Market

Employment, wages and unemployment — the labour-market direction of the work programme. Employment is tied to output through an Okun-type relation; wages are set against consumer prices through a Phillips-type relation; together they describe a tight but administratively-driven market.

How the labour market is modelled

Employment is linked to output by an Okun-type relation, estimated in growth rates: the change in log employment is regressed on real output growth, so the slope is the employment–output elasticity.

$$ \Delta\log(\mathrm{emp}_t)=\alpha+\beta\,g_t+\varepsilon_t $$(L1)

where Δlog(empt) is the growth of total employment, gt real GDP growth, α the autonomous component, β the employment–output elasticity and εt the disturbance. The estimated elasticity is β = 0.38 (R² = 0.40): about 0.38 of a point of employment growth per point of output growth, the modest pass-through one expects in an economy where the oil sector adds little to headcount.

Nominal wages are modelled with a wage-Phillips relation in growth rates, with inflation and a lagged wage-growth term allowing some persistence:

$$ \Delta\log(w_t)=c+\varphi\,\pi_t+\rho\,\Delta\log(w_{t-1})+\varepsilon_t $$(L2)

where Δlog(wt) is nominal wage growth, πt consumer-price inflation, Δlog(wt−1) its own lag, c the constant, φ the inflation pass-through, ρ the persistence and εt the disturbance. The fit is negligible — R² = 0.01, with φ ≈ 0.07 and ρ ≈ -0.05 both close to zero — so the equation carries almost no information and the wage path is taken from the Ministry's plan rather than forecast from inflation.

Wages and unemployment

Nominal wages have climbed steadily, but the wage–inflation link is weak: Azerbaijani wage growth has been driven more by discrete public-sector pay decisions than by consumer prices, so the equation is near-uninformative and the projection follows the Ministry's path. Real wage growth is modestly positive (+3.3 per cent in 2030). Unemployment edges down from 5.3 to 5.0 per cent — a broadly tight market.

What the forecast says

The picture is one of a tight, slowly tightening labour market. Employment rises to roughly 5440 thousand by 2030; the unemployment rate eases from 5.3 per cent in 2025 to 5.0 per cent in 2030; and real wages grow modestly, at about 3 per cent a year. There is little slack to absorb, so the gains from further output growth show up more in pay and participation than in large falls in unemployment.

Critical evaluation
Three caveats bound this block. The wage equation's near-zero fit (R² = 0.01) is itself the finding: nominal pay in Azerbaijan is set largely by administrative decision, not by a market wage-Phillips mechanism, so it is not forecastable from inflation alone and the projection simply follows the Ministry's wage plan. The short annual sample — barely a dozen observations — limits the precision of both regressions and widens the employment fan. And employment is mechanically tied to the (Ministry) growth path through the Okun elasticity, so it inherits that path's assumptions wholesale: if growth disappoints, so does employment, with no independent labour-supply or wage-bargaining channel to cushion it. The projection is best read as a consistent central path with appropriately wide bands, not a point prediction.
