Inflation and Monetary

Inflation in Azerbaijan is principally an external, pass-through process. This section sets out the projection and its uncertainty, decomposes inflation into its drivers, and reports the dynamic response to an oil-price shock.

The projection

The baseline path rises from the 5.6 per cent estimated for 2025 towards 7.2 per cent by 2030. The IMF World Economic Outlook is materially higher in the near term, which reflects a divergence worth noting: the Ministry's forecast for 2025, prepared in 2024, was 1.7 per cent, whereas the IMF now estimates 5.6 per cent for the same year. The 2025 outturn from the State Statistics Committee should be ingested to resolve this.

The drivers of inflation

The estimated equation decomposes inflation into the contributions of persistence, the exchange rate, world food prices and oil. The 2008 and 2022 episodes are dominated by world food prices, and the 2015–16 episode by the exchange rate following the devaluation. This composition is the basis for the pass-through coefficient of approximately 0.21 (bounded by the endogeneity analysis to roughly 0.10–0.21) reported in the Regression Results.

The static relationship and its dynamics

$$ \pi_t=\phi\,\pi_{t-1}+(1-\phi)\,\pi^{e}_t+\kappa\,\hat y_t+\theta\big(\Delta s_t+\Delta p^{*}_t\big)+\varepsilon_t $$(8)

The static equation above is estimated in the Regression Results; its dynamic counterpart is the vector-autoregression impulse response, under which a one-standard-deviation oil-price shock raises inflation by approximately 2.2 percentage points on impact before dissipating (Impulse Responses).

The exchange rate is the central channel
A large share of consumption is imported and the exchange rate is managed; movements in the parity, as in 2015–16, transmit to prices rapidly. The exchange rate is therefore a primary scenario control, and money-demand equations support the consistency of the monetary survey rather than the headline projection.
