External Sector

Exports, imports, the current account and the exchange rate. Azerbaijan's goods exports are overwhelmingly hydrocarbons, so the external accounts, like the budget, are an oil story; the manat is held on a de facto dollar peg. Oxlon estimates the trade elasticities and projects the current account against the IMF and the Ministry.

The external accounts in ratios

Read as shares of output, Azerbaijan's external position is strong but oil-dependent. The current-account balance swings with the oil price, from double-digit surpluses in boom years to near-balance when crude is weak, and stood at about 6 per cent of GDP in 2024 on World Bank data. Official reserves were around USD 13 billion, a comfortable buffer for the peg at current prices. These ratios frame the level forecast that follows.

How the trade flows are modelled

The two sides of the goods balance are estimated separately, each in log-differences so the coefficients read directly as elasticities. Imports are modelled on domestic demand, the growth of real imports responding to output growth, the slope giving the income elasticity of imports:

$$ \Delta\log(M_t)=\alpha+\eta\,g_t+\varepsilon_t $$(X1)

where Mt is the value of goods imports, gt output growth and η the income elasticity of imports, η = 2.05 (R² = 0.21).

Exports are modelled on the oil price, reflecting the roughly ninety per cent hydrocarbon share of the export basket, the slope giving the oil-price elasticity of exports:

$$ \Delta\log(X_t)=\alpha+\theta\,\Delta\log(\mathit{oil}_t)+\varepsilon_t $$(X2)

where Xt is the value of goods exports, oilt the oil price and θ the oil-price elasticity of exports, θ = 1.15 with a strong fit (R² = 0.91).

Goods exports track the oil price almost one-for-one, confirming the hydrocarbon share; imports rise with domestic demand at an income elasticity of 2.05. Feeding the Oxlon oil and growth paths through, the trade surplus narrows and the current account drifts towards balance.

The current account: Oxlon against the IMF and the Ministry

The two relations pull in opposite directions across the horizon. Exports track the oil price almost one-for-one (θ ≈ 1.15), so receipts move with the assumed price path; imports rise by roughly two per cent for each point of output growth (η ≈ 2.05), so the demand-led side keeps climbing. With the oil contribution fading, the Oxlon current account drifts from its early surplus towards balance and a small deficit of about +0.7 billion by 2030, while the Ministry holds a persistent surplus of about +2.2 billion. The IMF Article IV path (Figure 10.3, gold) sits between the two, a surplus that narrows steadily towards 1 per cent of GDP by 2030: an independent check that brackets the Oxlon and Ministry views.

The exchange rate

De facto US-dollar peg. After the two 2015 devaluations the Central Bank has held the manat at about 1.70 AZN/USD, managing the rate against the dollar rather than floating it. The rate has sat at about 1.70 manat per dollar since the 2015 devaluations, with a coefficient of variation since 2017 of just 0.004, effectively fixed. Oil-contingent and asymmetric. The peg is underwritten by hydrocarbon export receipts and SOFAZ reserves, so a sustained low oil price would drain reserves and strain the parity, as in 2015 when a collapse in the oil price forced two step devaluations; at current and higher oil prices the peg is comfortably financed.

The external risk
The trade and current-account surpluses, like the budget, rest on the oil price. The peg is comfortably financed at current prices but was devalued in 2015 when oil collapsed; a sustained low price is the central external risk, and it would transmit to inflation through the pass-through estimated in the Inflation section.
Critical evaluation
Three caveats bound the external projection. The imports relation fits poorly (R² = 0.21): import swings are driven by the investment and oil cycle, large project phases and capital goods, not by headline output growth, so the income elasticity is best read as indicative. Oxlon and the Ministry genuinely disagree on the current account: Oxlon turns it negative by about 2027 as oil receipts fade, against the Ministry's persistent surplus, a real forecast divergence worth flagging to the budget process rather than reconciling away, and the IMF path lies between them. And the exchange-rate peg is characterised, not estimated: its break risk is oil-contingent and sharply non-linear, quiescent at moderate prices then a step devaluation when reserves are exhausted, as in 2015, which a linear model cannot capture.
