Output and Growth

The output projection combines a sectoral, production-side build-up with the national-accounts identities. This section reports the baseline path and its uncertainty, the composition of growth, and the position of the economy in the cycle.

How output is modelled

Output is built from the supply side and tied together by the national-accounts identities. Total real output is the sum of the oil-and-gas and non-oil sectors, and its growth is the share-weighted sum of the two sectoral growth rates:

$$ Y_t=Y^{oil}_t+Y^{non}_t,\qquad g_t=s^{oil}_{t-1}\,g^{oil}_t+\big(1-s^{oil}_{t-1}\big)\,g^{non}_t $$(G1)

where Y is real value added, g the real growth rate, soil the oil sector's share of output and the superscripts denote the oil-and-gas and non-oil sectors; each sector's nominal value added is its real path times a sector deflator.

The non-oil sector — the engine of the forecast — is governed by an error-correction relation: output returns to a long-run path set by the capital stock, the real exchange rate and external demand, correcting deviations at a speed λ. The oil sector follows an exogenous production profile as mature fields decline.

$$ \Delta y^{non}_t=\alpha+\textstyle\sum_i \beta_i\,\Delta x_{i,t}-\lambda\big(y^{non}_{t-1}-\gamma'x_{t-1}\big)+\varepsilon_t $$(G2)

where ynon is log non-oil output, x the long-run determinants (capital, the real exchange rate, partner demand), β the short-run responses, γ the long-run elasticities and λ the speed of adjustment.

The baseline and its uncertainty

Real output growth is projected by aggregating sectoral value added, with the identities above enforced. The fan in Figure 8.1 sets the central path against 50 and 80 per cent probability bands and against the IMF World Economic Outlook. The Ministry's baseline is more optimistic than the IMF's for the near term — 2026 growth of 2.5 per cent against the IMF's lower path — a difference that matters for revenue planning and is examined in the Scenario Analysis.

The composition of growth

Growth is decomposed into the contributions of the oil-and-gas and non-oil sectors, each weighted by its share in output. The non-oil economy is the sole engine of growth across the horizon; the oil sector subtracts from growth as mature fields decline, contributing -0.2 percentage points in 2030. This is the central structural fact of the outlook and the reason the non-oil base is the focus of policy.

The cyclical position

Potential output and the output gap are estimated with a Hodrick-Prescott filter, which extracts the trend τ minimising the deviation of output from trend subject to a penalty on changes in the trend's slope; the gap is the residual:

$$ \min_{\{\tau_t\}}\ \sum_t (y_t-\tau_t)^2+\lambda\sum_t\big[(\tau_{t+1}-\tau_t)-(\tau_t-\tau_{t-1})\big]^2,\qquad \mathrm{gap}_t=100\,(y_t-\tau_t) $$(G3)

where yt is log non-oil output, τt the latent trend (potential output) and λ the smoothing parameter (100 for annual data, per the IMF convention). A Kalman-filter alternative is reported, as a cross-check, in the Bayesian and State-Space section.

The resulting gap is economically coherent — a trough of −7.4 per cent in 2020 and recovery to slightly above trend by 2024 — providing a consistent basis for the inflation and revenue projections.

Critical evaluation
Three caveats bound the projection. It reproduces the Ministry's published path by construction, so it inherits that path's optimism relative to the IMF — about half a point in 2026, which the scenarios stress-test. The short annual sample of about twenty-five observations limits the precision of the non-oil error-correction estimates and widens the fan. And the oil sector is an exogenous production profile, not a behavioural model, so it does not respond to price within this block — that linkage is supplied by the scenario engine and the structural VAR. The projection is best read as a consistent central path with honestly wide bands, not a point prediction.
