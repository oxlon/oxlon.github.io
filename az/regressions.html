Regression Results

The inflation equation is estimated as a sequence of specifications, in the manner of a conventional specification table, so that the contribution of each regressor and the stability of the estimates can be judged. The dependent variable is annual consumer-price inflation; the sample is 2000–2024; standard errors are heteroskedasticity-consistent.

Why several specifications

A single equation cannot establish that a coefficient is robust or that the relationship is not an artefact of the regressors included. Reporting a sequence — adding one block of variables at a time, and then varying the sample and the treatment of potentially endogenous regressors — shows which results are stable and isolates the concerns, principally simultaneity between the exchange rate and inflation, that bear on a causal interpretation.

Interpretation

Persistence alone (column 1) explains only fifteen per cent of the variation. Adding the exchange rate (column 2) contributes little, but adding the world food price (column 3) raises the fit sharply, to sixty-two per cent: imported prices, and food in particular, are the dominant proximate driver of Azerbaijani inflation. Once the exchange rate and food prices are present, the oil price (column 4) and broad money (column 5) add essentially nothing, which indicates that they operate through those channels rather than directly. The exchange-rate coefficient is stable at about 0.20 to 0.22 across columns (3) to (5).

Endogeneity and robustness

The contemporaneous exchange rate is plausibly determined jointly with inflation, which would bias its coefficient. Replacing it with its lagged value reduces the estimate to about 0.10, so a portion of the contemporaneous coefficient reflects simultaneity; the causal pass-through is therefore bounded between approximately 0.10 and 0.21. The oil price, by contrast, is exogenous to a price-taking economy and serves as a clean external regressor. The relationship is stronger in the post-2008 sub-sample (R² of 0.72) and is unaffected by the use of Newey–West standard errors, which indicates that the principal findings are not driven by the early-transition years or by residual serial correlation.

Critical evaluation

Three limitations qualify these estimates. First, twenty-five annual observations limit precision and preclude the full specification with an output gap, which is available only from 2013. Second, the 2015 devaluation is a structural break that a production specification would represent with an indicator variable, as the deployed equation does. Third, in-sample fit overstates forecasting accuracy: the out-of-sample comparison in the Machine-Learning section shows that the multivariate linear specification does not forecast well, which is why the projection relies on a combination of models and on the monthly panel rather than on this equation alone.
