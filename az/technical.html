Technical Report

A complete account of the methods, results and their interpretation, and the boundaries of each model's validity. Every figure is reproducible from the accompanying code against the Ministry's data.

1 · Data and identities

The dataset comprises the statutory tables under clause 2.4.1 (9,356 observations, 2013–2025 actual and 2026–2030 projected), an annual macroeconomic block from 1991, a monthly panel of 360 observations (prices, money, interest rates and Brent to April 2026), and the IMF World Economic Outlook as an independent benchmark. Two accounting identities were verified on the Ministry's data: output as the sum of oil and non-oil value added (maximum error 0.05 per cent) and the nominal reconstruction from real growth and the deflator (0.00 per cent). The engine reproduces the official medium-term path. The 2025 outturns published by the State Statistical Committee in January 2026 are incorporated as the last actual year, with 2026 the first forecast year; the verified 2025 figures (real GDP growth 1.4 per cent, average inflation 5.6 per cent) replace the earlier estimates throughout the report.

2 · Inflation: a specification sequence

The inflation equation is reported as a sequence of specifications (Regression Results). Persistence alone explains only about sixteen per cent of the variation; adding world food prices raises the fit to 63%, identifying imported prices as the dominant proximate driver, while oil and money add little once food and the exchange rate are present. The exchange-rate coefficient is stable at about 0.21 across specifications, but falls to about 0.10 when the regressor is lagged, evidence of simultaneity that bounds the causal pass-through to roughly 0.10–0.21 percentage points per one per cent depreciation. Explanatory fit and forecast accuracy are distinct objectives: the out-of-sample evidence (section 4) shows the multivariate linear specification forecasts poorly, so the projection relies on a model combination rather than this equation alone.

3 · Impulse responses and variance decomposition

A structural vector autoregression over 2000–2025 (26 observations), identified recursively in the order oil price → exchange rate → inflation, gives an impact inflation response of 2.2 percentage points to a one-standard-deviation oil shock, decaying within about three years; the full response matrix and bands are on the Impulse Responses page. The variance decomposition attributes about 41 per cent of the ten-year-ahead variance of inflation to oil and exchange-rate shocks jointly, and roughly 55 per cent of exchange-rate variance to the oil shock, a quantitative statement of the economy's external, oil-driven character. With this short annual sample the bands are wide; the quarterly five-variable model is the refinement set out in the Limitations.

4 · Forecast performance

Every requested model was evaluated by expanding-window walk-forward testing. The most accurate annual model is Neural Net (MLP) (root-mean-square error 3.12; skill +38% over a random walk), with a univariate time-series model and tree-based ensembles close behind and the over-parameterised linear models recording negative skill. On the monthly series a long short-term memory network forecasts inflation twelve months ahead with a root-mean-square error of 4.547 against 6.678 for persistence, a reduction of 32%. The determinant ranking places world food prices, the exchange rate and oil foremost, corroborating the structural relationship.

5 · Structural models

The three-equation New-Keynesian model returns correctly-signed responses to all three shocks (a monetary tightening lowers output and inflation; a cost-push shock generates the output-inflation trade-off; a demand shock raises both). The input-output block yields the Leontief inverse and propagates an oil final-demand shock through inter-industry linkages. These are theory-consistent cross-checks; the model is calibrated rather than estimated and the supply-use coefficients are illustrative pending the official table.

6 · Scenarios and the simulator

The oil price enters as a parameter rather than a forecast, with three statutory paths (baseline USD 65, conservative −15 per cent, stress −30 per cent). The simulator extends this to seven levers and propagates them, through documented first-order channels, to the 2025–2030 paths of output, inflation, the fiscal balance, public debt and the current account, with debt accumulating the implied deficits. A sustained oil price of USD 50, for instance, erodes the fiscal surplus and lifts the debt ratio materially by 2030, the central fiscal risk made explicit.

7 · Estimation conventions and reproducibility

Error-correction specifications require unit-root and cointegration testing; the medium-term core is calibrated and validated; the vector autoregression is identified recursively; machine-learning models are judged out of sample. Acceptance requires reproduction of the official forecast and a non-oil growth root-mean-square error below 1.5 percentage points before extension. Re-estimation is annual, timed ahead of the September submission, with a monthly refresh during execution. The entire analysis regenerates from the source data with a single command.
