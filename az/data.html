Data Foundation and Provenance

This section is the governed data foundation for the forecasting system. It documents the legal mandate, Ministry model assets, CAEM workbook, public institutional data, licensed market data, derived variables, validation checks and produced model panels that make each forecast traceable.

Purpose and scope

The data layer is the audit trail of the forecasting system. Every forecast, scenario and statutory table is linked from a legal or methodological requirement to a source series, transformation and model equation. The current evidence base is complete for the macro-fiscal forecasting and demonstration scope: Decree-75 requirements, the Ministry's CAEM and macroeconometric workbooks, official statistical sources, licensed market data, processed model panels and documented transformations.

Model-ready series and panels

The headline macroeconomic series now span 1991–2025 — real GDP growth, the GDP deflator, CPI inflation, population and the oil price, sourced from the World Bank, IMF and FRED and spliced with the Ministry's data — with the sectoral detail (the oil/non-oil decomposition and deflators under clause 2.4.1) available from 2013; the canonical forecast extends to 2031. The statutory tables contribute 9,356 observations. The high-frequency dataset comprises six monthly series from 1995, used for the inflation and monetary analysis. In addition, the public/licensed source archive contains more than 1,700 raw files across SSC, CBAR, Customs, MoF, SOFAZ, FRED, ECB, IMF, WRDS/CRSP and Refinitiv/LSEG.

Construction and transformations

Output is built from the production side: real value added is projected by sector and aggregated, and nominal value added follows from the sectoral deflators, with the headline identities enforced. The monthly consumer price series is a month-on-month index (100 denotes no change); it is cumulated to a price level from which the year-on-year inflation rate used in estimation is derived. Monetary aggregates and domestic rates are taken from CBAR; foreign policy rates come from FRED/ECB; Brent enters as a scenario parameter; and sovereign risk is constructed from bond yields rather than assumed.

$$ spread_t = YTM^{AZE}_t - YTM^{UST, matched}_t $$(D1)

Sovereign risk premium. The primary series is built from Refinitiv/LSEG Azerbaijan sovereign Eurobond yield-to-maturity data for XS1678623064 and XS1678623734, matched to US Treasury constant-maturity benchmarks. WRDS/CRSP data are used for benchmark validation and robustness.

$$ P^{m}_t=\Big(\sum_i w_i\,P^{world}_{i,t}\Big)\,e_t,\qquad \pi^m_t=\Delta\log P^{m}_t $$(D2)

Import-price index. The model-ready import-price input is a trade-weighted import deflator on Azerbaijan's import structure — manufactures (machinery, vehicles, chemicals, metals; w ≈ 0.68), food (≈ 0.18) and energy (≈ 0.14) world-price indices Pworld, expressed in manat e under the de-facto peg. This is the standard import-deflator construction, grounded in the World Bank / IMF commodity series and the SSC import structure.

$$ \pi^{core}_t=\pi^{\,\text{non-food}+\text{services}}_t $$(D3)

Core / base inflation. The model-ready core input is the ex-food core taken directly from the SSC consumer-price breakdown — the “non-food products & services” component (2.9 per cent in 2024) — reported beside CBAR's official base-inflation readings (4.4 per cent in 2024) as the validation anchor. The two differ by construction (CBAR's base excludes administered and seasonal items); both are carried in the system.

Currency of the data

The official 2025 outturns, published by the State Statistical Committee in January 2026, are incorporated: 2025 is the last actual year and 2026 the first forecast year, and the verified 2025 figures replace the earlier estimates throughout. The forecast for 2026 onwards is the project's own — a skill-weighted ensemble for inflation and non-oil growth, with headline GDP built from the oil/non-oil identity — estimated through the 2025 actual. The table below sets that forecast against the IMF World Economic Outlook as an external check; growth tracks the IMF closely while inflation runs a little higher, as documented in the benchmark discussion.

Live feeds that deepen the system

The data layer is complete for the macro-fiscal forecasting, scenario and statutory scope; the banking block runs on CBAR + IMF FSI/BSI, and the import-price and core/base inflation inputs are built and in use. Every model input should carry five fields: original source, native frequency, transformation, model owner and vintage. This is the acceptance test for the data layer. If a series is derived, the formula must be stored beside the output.

Conclusion
The evidence base covers the Ministry's model assets, the CAEM system, public institutional sources, licensed market data, banking drivers, derived price and core-inflation inputs, and produced econometric outputs. The macro-fiscal model is complete on the public-data foundation; the MİİS feeds add more granular execution, quarterly identification and regional reporting as they flow in.
