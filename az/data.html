Data Foundation and Provenance

This section combines the model data foundation with the provenance work behind it. It documents the assembled series, their sources and transformations, the Ministry workbooks and CAEM assets, the public and licensed data collection, the derived variables, and the validation checks that make each forecast traceable.

Purpose and scope

The data task is broader than downloading macroeconomic series. Every forecast, scenario and statutory table must be traceable from a legal or methodological requirement to a source series, transformation and model equation. This page therefore records the full data chain: Decree-75 requirements, the Ministry's CAEM and macroeconometric workbooks, official statistical sources, licensed market data, processed model panels and documented transformations.

Series held

The headline macroeconomic series now span 1991–2025 — real GDP growth, the GDP deflator, CPI inflation, population and the oil price, sourced from the World Bank, IMF and FRED and spliced with the Ministry's data — with the sectoral detail (the oil/non-oil decomposition and deflators under clause 2.4.1) available from 2013; the canonical forecast extends to 2031. The statutory tables contribute 9,356 observations. The high-frequency dataset comprises six monthly series from 1995, used for the inflation and monetary analysis. In addition, the public/licensed source archive contains 1,601 manifest records and 1,680 raw files across SSC, CBAR, Customs, MoF, SOFAZ, FRED, ECB, IMF, WRDS/CRSP and Refinitiv/LSEG.

Construction and transformations

Output is built from the production side: real value added is projected by sector and aggregated, and nominal value added follows from the sectoral deflators, with the headline identities enforced. The monthly consumer price series is a month-on-month index (100 denotes no change); it is cumulated to a price level from which the year-on-year inflation rate used in estimation is derived. Monetary aggregates and domestic rates are taken from CBAR; foreign policy rates come from FRED/ECB; Brent enters as a scenario parameter; and sovereign risk is constructed from bond yields rather than assumed.

$$ spread_t = YTM^{AZE}_t - YTM^{UST, matched}_t $$(D1)

Sovereign risk premium. The primary series is built from Refinitiv/LSEG Azerbaijan sovereign Eurobond yield-to-maturity data for XS1678623064 and XS1678623734, matched to US Treasury constant-maturity benchmarks. WRDS/CRSP data are used for benchmark validation and robustness.

$$ UV_t = \frac{\sum_i V_{i,t}}{\sum_i Q_{i,t}},\qquad \pi^m_t = \Delta\log(UV_t) $$(D2)

Import-price proxy. A clean official import-price index was not confirmed as a public long series. In this system the model-ready input is the derived unit-value / partner-price proxy, built from customs and trade value/quantity data and cross-checked against partner-price and exchange-rate indicators.

$$ \pi^{core}_t \approx \pi_t - \pi^{administered}_t - \pi^{seasonal}_t $$(D3)

Core/base inflation. CBAR publishes official core inflation as point readings in monetary-policy material rather than as a long machine-readable series. In this system the model-ready input is the derived continuous monthly proxy from CPI components, reported with a definition caveat because CBAR's base inflation excludes administered and seasonal items.

Currency of the data

The official 2025 outturns, published by the State Statistical Committee in January 2026, are incorporated: 2025 is the last actual year and 2026 the first forecast year, and the verified 2025 figures replace the earlier estimates throughout. The forecast for 2026 onwards is the project's own — a skill-weighted ensemble for inflation and non-oil growth, with headline GDP built from the oil/non-oil identity — estimated through the 2025 actual. The table below sets that forecast against the IMF World Economic Outlook as an external check; growth tracks the IMF closely while inflation runs a little higher, as documented in the benchmark discussion.

Remaining precision and internal-data requirements

The project no longer has a general public-data shortage. Banking is treated as ready at the current macro-financial model level using CBAR, IMF FSI/BSI and the Ministry monetary-accounting materials. Derived import-price and core/base inflation inputs are also treated as ready model inputs. The remaining items below affect precision or production-level deployment, but do not block the public-data prototype.

Every model input should carry five fields: original source, native frequency, transformation, model owner and vintage. This is the acceptance test for the data layer. If a series is derived, the formula must be stored beside the output; if a source is internal, the page should say so without weakening the public-data prototype.

Conclusion
The evidence base now covers the Ministry's model assets, the CAEM system, public institutional sources, licensed market data, banking drivers, derived price and core-inflation inputs, and produced econometric outputs. The remaining constraints are precision improvements and any internal production records requested later, not a broad inability to obtain public data.
