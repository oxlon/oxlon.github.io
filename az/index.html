Medium-term macroeconomic forecasting under the Economic Management Information System

This report sets out the macroeconomic forecasting component of the Ministry's Economic Management Information System, developed under the work programme established by Cabinet of Ministers Decree No. 75. It documents the assembled data, the estimated models, the forecast across the prescribed directions, and the scenario and risk analysis required for the preparation of the State Budget.

The central finding

The economy and the public finances are structurally sound but acutely oil-dependent, and that single dependence is the source of nearly all the medium-term risk. Estimated independently — from the structural vector autoregression, the fiscal model competition, the scenario engine and the computable general-equilibrium model — the oil price is the one factor that moves output, inflation, the budget and welfare together. The growth and fiscal positions are comfortable at a moderate oil price; both deteriorate quickly if it falls. The two instruments that address this — the non-oil diversification already under way and a binding fiscal rule — are therefore not refinements but the direct answer to the system's one structural vulnerability.

The medium-term forecast

Real output grows by about 2.5 per cent a year to 2030, led by the non-oil economy, which reaches 81 per cent of output as oil and gas production matures. Inflation is moderate, the budget holds a surplus and public debt is stable near 20 per cent of GDP.

The principal risk — the oil price

The fiscal surplus is, in substance, oil revenue: general-government revenue rises about 1.1 points of GDP for every ten per cent on the oil price, and oil is the dominant predictor of the balance. The scenarios make the exposure concrete — at about USD 45 the surplus is erased and debt doubles; at USD 35 the budget runs a deficit and debt approaches sixty per cent of output.

The general-equilibrium model puts the whole-economy cost beyond the budget: a thirty per cent fall in the oil price leaves real output little changed in quantity but cuts real income by about 9 and household welfare by about 12 per cent through the terms of trade, as labour and capital reallocate out of oil into the non-oil sector.

What drives inflation

Inflation is largely imported, and therefore controllable through the exchange-rate anchor. Decomposing its forecast-error variance, the exchange rate accounts for about 22 per cent and the oil price about 18 per cent — some 40 per cent external — with the remainder its own persistence. A one per cent depreciation of the manat raises consumer prices by about 0.21 percentage points.

What forecasts best

What forecasts best depends on frequency. On the short annual sample parsimony wins; the value of machine learning rises sharply with data frequency and horizon, where the monthly models deliver large gains over a no-change benchmark. The system is configured accordingly — structural and parsimonious models for the annual statutory core, machine learning at monthly and, prospectively, transaction frequency.

Scope and method

The terms of reference under Decree No. 75 specify ten forecast directions, a defined set of econometric and machine-learning models, and four cross-cutting capabilities. This work consolidates the Ministry's sectoral models and Financial Programming framework into a single reproducible system, re-estimates the behavioural relationships against the data, and implements the full requested model suite — classical time-series, Bayesian and large Bayesian VARs, error-correction and state-space (Kalman) models, the DSGE, input-output and computable general-equilibrium models, and the machine-learning and deep-learning forecasters. The system reproduces the official 2025–2030 projection; every result on this page is reproducible from the accompanying code and the Ministry's source data.

Each finding is documented, with diagnostics, in the sections that follow — the forecast in Output, Inflation and Fiscal; the drivers in Regression Results and Impulse Responses; the model evidence in Machine-Learning, Bayesian and State-Space, and Structural Models; and the constraints in Limitations and Data Requirements.
