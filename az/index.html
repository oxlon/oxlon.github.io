Medium-term macroeconomic forecasting under the Economic Management Information System

This summary sets out the macroeconomic forecasting component of the Ministry's Economic Management Information System, prepared under Cabinet of Ministers Decree No. 75. It states the headline forecast and its drivers, places that forecast against the IMF and the Ministry's own CAEM model, and identifies the single risk that dominates the outlook. The data, the estimated models and the diagnostics behind every figure are documented in the sections that follow, and every number is reproducible from the accompanying code and the Ministry's source data.

The central finding

The economy and the public finances are sound but heavily dependent on oil, and that dependence accounts for most of the medium-term risk. The same conclusion arises from four separate models, the structural vector autoregression, the fiscal model competition, the scenario engine and the computable general-equilibrium model, each of which identifies the oil price as the factor that moves output, inflation, the budget and household welfare together. Growth and the budget are comfortable at a moderate oil price and weaken quickly when it falls. The two instruments that address this, the non-oil diversification already under way and a binding fiscal rule, are therefore the direct answer to the system's main vulnerability rather than secondary refinements.

The medium-term forecast

Real output grows by about 2.3 per cent a year through 2030, led by the non-oil economy, whose share of output rises from 72 to 81 per cent as oil and gas production matures. Inflation eases from the 5.6 per cent recorded in 2025 towards 5.3 per cent, the budget stays in surplus and public debt holds near 20 per cent of GDP. The year 2025 is the last actual outturn and 2026 the first forecast year.

The sector forecasts fill in the same picture. On the Okun's-law equation the unemployment rate eases from 5.3 per cent in 2025 to 4.8 per cent by 2030 (the IMF projects 5.0); gross fixed investment keeps rising in level but slips as a share of output, from about 17 to 16 per cent of GDP, as the post-construction oil economy matures; the current account drifts from surplus towards balance, to about USD +0.7 billion on the Oxlon path by 2030 against the Ministry's USD +2.2 billion, the gap being how fast oil receipts are taken to fade; and a slowing population, near 10.3 million by 2030, lifts income per head faster than output. Each is estimated and documented on its own page (Labour, Investment, External Sector, Demography), every series anchored on the 2025 actual.

How the forecast compares with the IMF and CAEM

Two different Ministry numbers — and why it matters
The Ministry has two forecasts that do not agree. CAEM is its IMF-built semi-structural model — the engine that produced the 2026 Budget — and its inflation converges to about 4 per cent. The Decree-75 statutory submission (the 8 vərəq tables) is a separate, more pessimistic path whose inflation rises to 7.2 per cent by 2030. They diverge: the Ministry's own submission departs from its own model. This summary benchmarks against CAEM as the model of record and the IMF; the live solver shows all three (Oxlon, CAEM, Decree-75) side by side. Oxlon reconciles to the IMF/CAEM family, not the Decree-75 outlier.

The Oxlon forecast pairs a skill-weighted ensemble — for inflation and non-oil growth — with the oil/non-oil identity for headline GDP, estimated through the 2025 outturn. Set against the two independent references, the growth path stays very close: the average distance from the IMF Article IV projection over 2026–2029 is about 0.1 of a percentage point on growth, while inflation runs about 0.9 of a point above the IMF, the ensemble carrying more of inflation's historical persistence than the IMF's faster return to target. An independent method that reproduces the IMF and CAEM growth picture and brackets their inflation is strong evidence the forecast is well founded.

Are the Oxlon, IMF and CAEM forecasts independent?
Yes. The three are produced separately. The IMF Article IV figures are the Fund's own staff projections, not an output of the Ministry's CAEM model. CAEM is the Ministry's IMF-style financial-programming model, built with IMF technical assistance, so it belongs to the same methodological family as the Fund's framework but is run by the Ministry on its own assumptions. The Oxlon forecast is a separate multi-model ensemble estimated from the published data. Because the three are built independently and still agree closely, the agreement is genuine corroboration rather than a shared starting point.

The principal risk: the oil price

The fiscal surplus is, for the most part, oil revenue. General-government revenue rises by about 1.1 points of GDP for every ten per cent on the oil price, and the oil price is the dominant predictor of the balance. The scenarios make the exposure concrete. At about USD 45 the surplus is erased and the debt ratio rises sharply, and at USD 35 the budget runs a deficit with debt approaching sixty per cent of output.

The general-equilibrium model shows the cost reaches beyond the budget. A thirty per cent fall in the oil price leaves the quantity of output little changed, because labour and capital move into the non-oil sector, but it cuts real national income by about 9 per cent and household welfare by about 12 per cent through the terms of trade.

What drives inflation

Inflation is largely imported and is therefore best managed through the exchange-rate anchor. Decomposing its forecast-error variance, the exchange rate accounts for about 22 per cent and the oil price about 19 per cent, so roughly 41 per cent is external in origin, with the remainder reflecting inflation's own persistence. A one per cent depreciation of the manat raises consumer prices by about 0.21 percentage points.

What forecasts best

The most accurate method depends on the frequency of the data. On the short annual sample the parsimonious models win and the elaborate ones overfit, so the headline forecast rests on transparent econometric models. At monthly frequency the larger sample supports the machine-learning and deep-learning models, which deliver clear gains over a no-change benchmark. The system is configured to match: structural and parsimonious models for the annual statutory core, machine learning at monthly and, in due course, transaction frequency.

How the Oxlon forecast is built

The Oxlon forecast is built two ways, each matched to the data. Inflation and non-oil growth are produced by a skill-weighted ensemble: for each indicator a panel of candidate models is estimated on the history through the 2025 actual — a first-order autoregression (AR1), an error-correction model (ECM), a structural potential-output model, a vector autoregression (VAR), a shrinkage Bayesian VAR (BVAR), and for inflation an expectations anchor — and combined by forecasting skill. Headline GDP is then built from the oil/non-oil identity set out below, so it rests on the non-oil ensemble and an explicit oil-sector path rather than a single time-series model. Machine-learning models (regularised regression, random forests, gradient boosting, neural nets) and a CAEM-style structural Phillips curve are run separately as cross-checks; on the short annual sample the machine-learning models overfit and add no out-of-sample skill.

Each model is scored by expanding-window, one-step-ahead back-testing over 2012–2024: at every year the model is re-estimated on earlier data only and made to predict the next, and its accuracy is summarised by the root-mean-square error (RMSE) against the outturn,

$$ \mathrm{{RMSE}}_m=\sqrt{{\tfrac1H\sum_{{h=1}}^{{H}}\bigl(\hat y_{{m,h}}-y_h\bigr)^2}} $$(1)

where \(\hat y_{m,h}\) is model \(m\)'s one-step forecast and \(y_h\) the realised value over the \(H\) test years. The forecast is then the weighted average of the models that beat a random walk, with weights inversely proportional to squared error, so a model that forecasts twice as accurately receives four times the weight:

$$ \hat y^{{\,\text{{Oxlon}}}}_t=\sum_{{m}} w_m\,\hat y_{{m,t}},\qquad w_m=\frac{{\mathrm{{RMSE}}_m^{{-2}}}}{{\sum_{{k}}\mathrm{{RMSE}}_k^{{-2}}}} $$(2)

This inverse-variance rule is the standard, theoretically optimal way to pool unbiased forecasts: under independent errors it is the combination that minimises the variance of the pooled forecast (Bates and Granger, 1969; Timmermann, 2006). Models with no skill against the random-walk benchmark are dropped, so the average is taken only over models that demonstrably help. For inflation the surviving weights are 52 per cent AR1 and 48 per cent Anchor — the autoregressive term and the expectations anchor; the exchange-rate pass-through error-correction model and the VARs do not beat the random walk on the annual sample and are dropped. For non-oil growth the autoregressive and potential-output models share the weight.

Headline GDP follows the oil/non-oil identity: real GDP growth is the value-added-share-weighted sum of oil and non-oil growth. Non-oil growth is the ensemble above (3.7 per cent on average over 2026–2030); the oil sector, whose real value added fell 1.6 per cent in 2025, is assumed to decline about 1.5 per cent a year as mature fields run down. The identity gives real GDP growth of 2.1 per cent in 2026 rising to 2.5 per cent by 2030. As an independent check, a five-model time-series ensemble on total GDP (weights 23 per cent Potential and 20 per cent AR1 and 20 per cent ECM and 18 per cent BVAR and 18 per cent VAR) back-tests 27 per cent more accurately than a random walk (RMSE 3.75 against 5.17) and gives a similar, marginally lower path (1.3–1.8 per cent), which corroborates the identity. For inflation the ensemble's back-tested RMSE is 4.49 against 5.38 for a random walk, a 17 per cent reduction. The 50 and 80 per cent fan bands shown above are built from the historical one-year-ahead error dispersion, widening with the horizon and then stabilising as growth reverts. Crucially, the whole procedure uses only data available before each test year, so the reported skill is genuine out-of-sample performance, not in-sample fit.

Finally the forecast is validated against two independent benchmarks: the IMF Article IV projection and the Ministry's own CAEM model. The Oxlon growth path reproduces the IMF picture to within 0.15 of a point on average over 2026–2029 (Figures 1.2–1.3), without being fitted to it; on inflation Oxlon runs about 0.9 of a point above the IMF, because the ensemble carries the historical persistence of inflation while the IMF assumes a faster return to the 4 per cent target. Close agreement on growth with an independently-constructed forecast is evidence the method is sound, and the inflation gap is small, explicable and transparent.
