Limitations and Data Requirements

The constraints on the present analysis and the data that would extend it. These are factual and bear directly on the accuracy and scope of the forecast.

Data limitations

The principal constraint is the length and frequency of the available series. The status column below records not whether a series exists (almost all of it does) but whether it is presently in the model, and if not, what single step would bring it in. Why longer and higher-frequency data matters

The annual sample of about twenty-five usable observations is the binding constraint on the precision of every multivariate estimate. It widens the impulse-response bands, limits the number of regressors that can be identified, leaves the exchange-rate pass-through bounded rather than point-identified, and prevents estimation of the quarterly structural model. Quarterly national accounts would roughly quadruple the effective sample and permit the five-variable model of the technical design; reliable, revision-managed series with consistent definitions across the statistical transitions of the 1990s and 2000s would further improve the estimates, and several early observations were excluded for this reason.

Model limitations

  • Small-sample imprecision. The vector autoregression and its variance decomposition rest on twenty-five annual observations; the responses are directionally reliable but the bands are wide, and the decomposition shares should be read as orders of magnitude.
  • Pass-through is bounded, not point-identified. Simultaneity between the exchange rate and inflation means the causal pass-through is estimated as a range (about 0.10–0.21); resolving it to a point requires an instrument, which the quarterly data would support.
  • The simulator is first-order. Its channels are proportional and contemporaneous: it omits non-linearities, dynamic feedback and regime changes, and the debt path simply accumulates the implied deficits. It is built for rapid, auditable what-if analysis; the dynamic and structural questions are the province of the vector autoregression and the DSGE.
  • Fan-chart bands are empirical. The probability intervals are derived from the historical dispersion of forecast errors, not from a structural predictive density; they are deliberately wide by 2030, reflecting the economy's dependence on the oil price.
  • The external benchmark is an independent comparator. The IMF World Economic Outlook is shown alongside the Oxlon forecast as an independent cross-check, constructed at a different date and on different assumptions; across the forecast horizon the two are nonetheless closely aligned (mean gaps of about 0.1 point on growth and 0.6 on inflation over 2026–29), which is documented on the Forecast Benchmark page.
  • Structural models are calibrated. The DSGE is calibrated at conventional values rather than estimated, and the input-output coefficients are illustrative pending the official supply-use table.
  • The oil-fund transfer is discretionary. The transfer from the State Oil Fund is set by decision, not formula; its representation as a function of the non-oil deficit is an approximation, disclosed in the fiscal outputs.
  • The oil price is not forecast. No model forecasts it reliably at budget-relevant horizons; the scenario approach is the appropriate response, and all three paths are reported together.
