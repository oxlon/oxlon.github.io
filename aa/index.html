1 Motivation & scope

Where the Real-Time module (15.7.2) answers "how is the economy doing now?", Advanced Analytics answers the forward and structural questions: where are the headline indicators heading (predictive modelling), which drivers carry the signal (machine learning), and what hidden structure the Ministry's data holds (data mining). All three run on the same harmonised store and are validated against conventional benchmarks.

2 Predictive modelling — 2026-2030 outlook

For each indicator a horse-race of conventional models (drift random walk, damped-trend exponential smoothing, ARIMA) is scored by a rolling-origin backtest; the best is refitted and forecast with an 80% band.

5 Methodology

Predictive (15.7.3.1)

Conventional, transparent time-series models in a horse-race selected by out-of-sample backtest, not in-sample fit. Forecast intervals widen with horizon. Benchmarked against a random walk so "skill" is honest. Relation to the flagship forecast: this is a deliberately different, lightweight statistical model-selection layer — it complements rather than replaces the structural NK-DSGE + 92-equation sectoral engine used in the Ministry report (Module 11.1). The two are mutually validating; where they diverge (statistical extrapolation of the recent slowdown vs the structural model's projected recovery) the gap is itself an informative signal.

Machine learning (15.7.3.2)

Supervised regression (gradient boosting, random forest, regularised linear) with time-series cross-validation to avoid look-ahead. Feature importance gives explainability; CV-drift defines the monitoring trigger.

Data mining (15.7.3.3)

Unsupervised: hierarchical (Ward) + k-means clustering on standardised regional profiles, PCA for visualisation, and pairwise correlation for association discovery.
